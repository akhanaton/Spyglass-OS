## The one message above all others: unsupported calculator answers

This is the single most repeated examiner message across **every paper, every session**. It is
the spine of the whole article.

- Working must justify the solution. A correct final answer with no method earns no marks — and
  even when the result is correct, it is "not acceptable" without working (s24 P13).
- Applies to: quadratics, trig equations, simultaneous equations, definite integration results,
  gradients, intersection points, and sums of series.
- For quadratics specifically: must show factorisation, the quadratic formula (with values
  **substituted in**, not just quoted), or completing the square — and factors must expand back
  to the original coefficients.
- A "significant minority" still lose these marks year on year (w22 P11, P12, P13).

**Quotable:**
- "You must show your working clearly; no marks will be given for unsupported answers from a calculator." (s23 / s24 / w22, Key messages — appears verbatim across years)
- "Using calculators to solve equations and writing down only the solution is not sufficient for certain marks to be awarded." (s24 P11; w21 P11)
- "It is also insufficient to quote only the formula: candidates need to show values substituted into it." (s24 P11)
- "Examiners are unable to give credit when ... quadratic equation solutions, definite integration results, gradients, simultaneous equation solutions, intersection points and sums of series arrive from little or no working. Even when these results are correct they are not acceptable." (s24 P13)
- "the use of a calculator function to solve the quadratic was not accepted and should be strongly discouraged." (w22 P12 Q3)

## TOPIC 1 — Circular Measure (the most consistent weak spot)

Appears every year and is the most reliable source of avoidable mark loss in Pure 1.

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **Radians vs degrees confusion** — converting to degrees mid-solution loses accuracy, and
   answers mixing the two "very rarely" gain credit. Working in radians throughout is the
   reliable route. (w22 P12 Q10; w21 P13 Q5; s23 P13 Q6)
2. **Cannot find the required angle from the given information → no progress.** If the candidate
   can't establish the angle, every downstream mark is lost. (w21 P6; w22 P11 Q5)
3. **Perimeter vs area, and segment vs sector confusion** — calculating a segment area when a
   perimeter is asked, or stopping after only the perimeter/only the area when both are needed.
   (s23 P11 Q4; s24 P11 Q7)
4. **Triangle-area errors** — using half the triangle instead of the whole, misquoting
   ½r²sin θ, or assuming an isosceles/equilateral triangle that isn't (e.g. assuming AB = r).
   (w21 P11 Q6; w22 P11 Q5; s23 P11 Q4 — "assuming triangle ABC was right-angled")
5. **Premature approximation of the angle** cascades into lost final-accuracy marks.
   (w20 P12 Q8; w22 P13 Q8)

**Mark-loss mechanism:** the angle is the gate. Get the angle wrong or in the wrong unit and the
whole question collapses. Segment area = sector area − triangle area is the formula candidates
most often misassemble.

**Cross-year:** consistently weak 2020–2024; the radian-vs-degree and segment-vs-sector errors
are the most durable.

**Quotable:**
- "It was very rare to see an answer confusing degrees and radians gain any credit." (w21 P13 Q3)
- "Those working in radians were more successful than those attempting to convert the angles to degrees, and this approach should be discouraged." (w22 P12 Q10)
- "Some candidates stopped after finding only the perimeter or area." (s24 P11 Q7)
- "A common error was to calculate half the area of the triangle rather than the whole area." (w21 P11 Q6)

## TOPIC 2 — Differentiation

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **Not recognising differentiation is required** — especially in rates-of-change questions,
   where candidates substitute a value directly into the original formula instead of
   differentiating. This is a zero-mark failure, not a slip. (s23 P11 Q9; w20 P11 Q3)
2. **Chain rule omitted or misapplied** — the most consistently lost technique, in both
   related-rates and function-of-a-function contexts. (w20 P11 Q3; w22 P12 Q11; w22 P13 Q4)
3. **Stationary points: failing to reject the invalid root**, and forgetting to find the
   corresponding y-value. The second-derivative test itself is generally understood. (w20 P13 Q8;
   s24 P11 Q11; w21 P13 Q8)
4. **"Always positive / always increasing" not actually proven** — substituting a few values is
   not a proof. (w21 P13 Q3; s23-era pattern)
5. **Index / power errors** — e.g. writing 1/(2x) as 2x⁻¹; mishandling fractional and negative
   powers. (w21 P13 Q5)

**Mark-loss mechanism:** recognition first (do I differentiate?), then technique (chain rule),
then housekeeping (reject invalid root, find y, justify nature of point).

**Cross-year:** rates-of-change recognition and the chain rule are the durable failures across
all sessions.

**Quotable:**
- "Use of the chain rule to connect the given and required rate of change was seen very occasionally." (w20 P11 Q3)
- "Careful reading of the question reveals there is only one stationary point, but many candidates did not reject one of the solutions." (w20 P13 Q8)
- "A significant number of candidates did not realise that the substitution of individual values into the gradient ... was not sufficient evidence to conclude that the gradient was always positive." (w21 P13 Q3)

## TOPIC 3 — Integration

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **Volume of revolution: forgetting to square y.** Candidates who don't square are left with
   an integral they cannot evaluate, or compute an area instead of a volume. (w21 P11 Q10; s23 P11 Q10; s24 P11 Q9)
2. **Area vs volume confusion** — reading "volume of revolution" and computing the area under
   the curve. (s24 P11 Q9; s23 P11 Q10)
3. **Constant of integration omitted** when finding the equation of a curve from its gradient —
   forfeits the final marks. (w21 P11 Q9; w20 P12 Q7)
4. **Definite integration: limits not shown substituted.** Both limits must visibly go into the
   expression before any calculator step. (w20 P12 Q10; s24 area questions)
5. **Integrate vs differentiate direction confusion** — power reduced instead of increased; and
   the reverse-chain-rule divisor (the coefficient of x) omitted. (s23 P11 Q10; w22 P11 Q2)
6. **Curve vs line** — integrating is skipped and a straight-line equation used instead.
   (w20 P12 Q4)

**Mark-loss mechanism:** for volumes, the square is the make-or-break step. For "find the curve",
the constant of integration is the recurring forfeit. For definite integrals, visible limit
substitution is the method-mark gate.

**Cross-year:** the "square y" omission in volume of revolution is remarkably durable across
years.

**Quotable:**
- "most candidates omitted to square the expression for y, as required by the formula for integration to find a volume of revolution." (w21 P11 Q10)
- "Those few who did not square y were then faced with an integral they could not find." (s23 P13 Q9)
- "Other candidates found the area under the curve rather than the volume of revolution and they are to be reminded to read each question carefully." (s24 P11 Q9)
- "It is important to remember that both limits in a definite integral need to be shown substituted into the expression." (w20 P12 Q10)

## TOPIC 4 — Quadratics & Algebra

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **Solving by calculator without showing method** (see calculator section) — the dominant
   mark-loss in every quadratic question.
2. **Discriminant questions: stopping at the discriminant.** Candidates form b²−4ac but cannot
   use completing-the-square (or similar) to show an expression is always positive / has two
   distinct roots for all values of a parameter. (w20 P12 Q3; s24 P10)
3. **Critical values given instead of solving the inequality** — leaving "−22, 2" rather than
   stating the inequality region. (w21 P11 Q2; w22 P13 Q4)
4. **Completing the square with a negative leading coefficient** — sign handling errors; not
   reversing the sign change afterward. (w21 P13 Q11)
5. **Ignoring "complete the square" instruction** — using another method caps available marks.
   (w21 P13 Q11)

**Mark-loss mechanism:** quadratics are where the calculator rubric bites hardest, and
discriminant questions reward the second step (interpretation) that most candidates skip.

**Quotable:**
- "several left their answer as −22, 2 rather than applying correct inequalities." (w21 P13 Q4)
- "Sign errors were common, leading to the wrong quadratic equation." (w21 P11 Q2)

## TOPIC 5 — Trigonometry (identities & equations)

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **Losing the secondary solution** — finding one root in range (often via calculator) and
   missing the second/third/fourth-quadrant solution, or including an out-of-range value.
   (s24 P11 Q5; w20 P11 Q7; w22 P11 Q6)
2. **Replacing tan with sin/cos when factorising in the original form would be cleaner** —
   creates far more work and few succeed. (w21 P11 Q3)
3. **Combining algebraic fractions** (common denominator) — multiplication and sign errors; a
   "fundamental skill" examiners repeatedly flag. (w20 P11 Q7; w20 P12 Q6)
4. **The "2x" / double-angle range not handled** — second angle found as 180−x instead of 90−x;
   forgetting to consider the full range for the multiple angle. (s23-era; w21 P13 Q4)
5. **Rounding the multiple-angle value** before dividing back, losing final accuracy.
   (w21 P13 Q4)

**Mark-loss mechanism:** range management. The technique is usually fine; the marks leak from
incomplete solution sets and from fraction algebra.

**Quotable:**
- "Candidates who wrote cos x = −1/4 were unable to gain any marks." (s24 P11 Q5)
- "the negative alternative was often ignored." (w20 P11 Q7)
- "Several candidates made errors in adding algebraic fractions. This is a fundamental skill which candidates would benefit from practicing more extensively." (w20 P12 Q6)

## TOPIC 6 — Functions & Inverses

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **Choosing the wrong square-root branch for the inverse** — not restricting to match the
   domain of the original function, so the wrong sign is kept. (s24 P11 Q6c; w21 P11 Q8d; w22 P13 Q2)
2. **Range stated incorrectly** — wrong inequality sign, single value instead of a range, or
   < vs ≤ confusion. (w21 P11 Q8c; w20 P12 Q4b)
3. **Not recognising a non-one-to-one function has no inverse** — and "fails the horizontal line
   test" alone is not a sufficient justification. (w22 P12 Q9c)
4. **Missing the "hence" hint linking parts** — a given/earlier part intended to scaffold the
   next is ignored. (w21 P13 Q6; w20 P11 Q8)
5. **Inverse-graph sketches omit y = x** and misposition the curve. (s24 P11 Q6a; w21 P13 Q6a)

**Mark-loss mechanism:** the inverse is where domain/range discipline is tested. Candidates who
treat it as pure algebra (without reference to the original domain) keep the wrong branch.

**Quotable:**
- "did not consider the two square roots of 4 and gave an answer of 2 rather than –2." (s24 P11 Q6c)
- "a comment that the function would fail the horizontal line test was insufficient to be awarded the mark." (w22 P12 Q9c)
- "It was rare to see the line y = x on the candidates' sketches." (w21 P13 Q6a)

## TOPIC 7 — Series (Arithmetic & Geometric Progressions)

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **AP/GP technique confusion** — applying AP formulae to a GP question (or vice versa) earns
   no credit. (w22 P11 Q7; w22 P12 Q2 & Q4)
2. **nth-term vs sum formula mix-up** — using the sum formula when the nth term is required, or
   vice versa. (s24 P11 Q8; w20 P11 Q4)
3. **Leaving the answer in terms of an unknown** (e.g. "1275a", "an expression containing p")
   instead of solving for the value. (w22 P12 Q2; w20 P12 Q2)
4. **r > 1 not sanity-checked** for sum to infinity — substituting an impossible ratio anyway.
   (w20 P12 Q2; w21 P10)
5. **"Exact" ignored** — giving a decimal when an exact form is required; not evaluating index
   expressions. (w20 P12 Q6)
6. **Series with trigonometric terms** consistently challenging. (w20 P11; w21 P12 Q5)

**Quotable:**
- "If this type of answer was expected, then the question would have asked for an answer in terms of a." (w22 P12 Q2)
- "Some candidates incorrectly obtained a value of r greater than 1 but often did not identify that an error had been made and substituted it into the formula anyway." (w20 P12 Q2)

## TOPIC 8 — Coordinate Geometry & Circles

(Circles were a **new topic** from the 2020 revised syllabus — weakest in 2020, improving since.)

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **Circle equation form errors** — forgetting to square the radius, sign errors in
   (x−a)²+(y−b)², or assuming "an equation" means a straight line. The standard form outperforms
   the expanded/general form. (w20 P11 Q9; w20 P12 Q9; w21 P7)
2. **Tangency via discriminant** — substituting the line into the circle and setting b²−4ac = 0
   is the reliable route; candidates who used y = mx + c made no progress. (s24 P10; w22 P11 Q11)
3. **Using the origin instead of the centre** of the circle for gradient/normal work.
   (w20 P12 Q12)
4. **Point inside/outside a circle: insufficient reasoning** — using ≠ (only shows "not on the
   circle") instead of > with an explicit "lies outside" conclusion. (w20 P13 Q11)
5. **Not drawing a diagram** — the most-omitted, lowest-scoring parts are repeatedly the ones
   where a sketch would have unlocked the method. (w20 P12 Q9; w21 P13 Q9; w22 P11 Q10b)

**Mark-loss mechanism:** circles reward the diagram and the centre. The two highest-omission
parts across the whole dataset are circle/tangent geometry questions.

**Quotable:**
- "This question was found to be the most demanding on the paper." (w20 P13 Q11)
- "In order to obtain the final mark, it was necessary to use the symbol > and to state the conclusion that T lies outside the circle." (w20 P13 Q11)
- "Using a diagram may well have avoided this waste and should always be encouraged." (s24 P12 Q7)
- "Candidates should be encouraged to draw appropriate sketch diagrams." (w21 P13, Key messages)

## TOPIC 9 — Binomial Expansion

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **Index errors on the bracket** — simplifying (ax)³ to a³x³ wrongly, or (ax)² to ax²; the
   single most common slip. (s24 P11 Q3; w22 P11 Q4)
2. **Forgetting to square/cube the constant** inside the bracket (e.g. the −2). (w20 P12 Q1)
3. **80a vs 80a²** type coefficient errors; multiplying the wrong coefficient. (s24 P12 Q1)
4. **Doing the full multiplication of both expansions** instead of isolating the required term —
   "introduces errors". (w21 P11 Q1)
5. **Omitting the leading term (1)** in the expansion. (w21 P11 Q1)

**Quotable:**
- "Weaker candidates sometimes either forgot to square and cube the (−2) from their expansions, only selected the term in x³ or could not form an equation." (w20 P12 Q1)

## TOPIC 10 — Transformations (also new in the 2020 syllabus)

**Top recurring mistakes:**
1. **Imprecise terminology** — "move" instead of "translation", "squeeze/compression" instead
   of "stretch", missing the word "factor", or omitting the direction. (w20 P12 Q11; s24 P12 Q2)
2. **Wrong / incomplete vector** for translations; "left"/"down"/"up" not accepted — must be a
   column vector or stated in x/y directions. (w21 P13 Q1; w20 P13 Q1)
3. **Order matters** — stretch-then-translate ≠ translate-then-stretch; candidates interchange
   them. (s23 P13 Q6; s24 P11 Q2)
4. **Wrong stretch factor / direction** — using factor 2 instead of ½, or the wrong axis.
   (w21 P11 Q5; w22 P12 Q5)
5. **Applying the transformation to the given point** instead of finding the original point on
   the curve. (w22 P12 Q2)

**Quotable:**
- "Words such as 'left' or 'down' were often seen but not accepted." (w20 P13 Q1)
- "Stretch (not squeeze or compression, for example) should always be given with the correct scale factor and with the direction clearly indicated. Translation (not move) should be given with a column vector." (w21 P12, Key messages)

## Cross-cutting themes (apply to every topic — strong article spine)

1. **Show your working / unsupported calculator answers** — the dominant message, every paper,
   every year. (See dedicated section above.)
2. **Accuracy & premature rounding** — work to ≥4 s.f. when 3 s.f. is required; "exact" means
   exact at every stage; 2 s.f. answers forfeit accuracy marks. Recurs in Circular Measure,
   Trig, Series. (w20/w22/w21 multiple)
3. **"Show that" / proof questions** — every step must be shown; do not assume the result and
   work backwards; jumping lines loses method marks. (w20 P12 & P13; w22 P11 Q6; w21 P13 Q7)
4. **"Hence" / linked parts** — earlier parts and given answers are deliberate scaffolding;
   re-deriving them wastes time and ignoring them loses the route. (s24 P12 Q6 & Q10; w22 P12 Q6;
   w21 P13 Q6)
5. **Read the question / use the whole function** — volume vs area, missing a term of the
   function, stopping after one of two required quantities, noting the marks available.
   (s24 P11 Q9; w20/s24 P9; s24 P11 Q7)
6. **Draw a diagram** — explicitly recommended; the highest-omission parts are the ones where a
   sketch unlocks the method. (Circles especially.)
7. **Presentation** — pencil overwritten in ink scans badly and can cost marks; write in dark
   ink. (w20 P12; w22 P12)

## Article angle recommendations (for Teresa's review → Phase 2)

These are not decisions, just what the data supports:

- **Strongest spine:** "What examiners actually say you lose marks for" — the calculator/working
  rubric + accuracy + show-that, framed as method discipline, not knowledge gaps. This is the
  learning-science insight: most marks are lost on *execution habits*, not *understanding*.
- **Strongest topic hook:** Circular Measure — most consistent, most quotable, and ties to the
  already-published Circular Measure article (internal link opportunity, supports EP-67 Pure 1
  pillar).
- **Credibility angle (E-A-T):** the 2020 syllabus change (Circles + Transformations new) gives
  a defensible "here's how the cohort adapted over five years" narrative — Teresa as analytical
  reviewer fits this framing well.
- **GEO / extractable structure:** each topic above is already an answer-first block with a
  mark-loss mechanism — maps cleanly to H2s with FAQ-style "Why do students lose marks on X?"
- **[VERIFY] discipline:** no numeric "you lose N marks" claims — examiner reports give
  qualitative patterns, not mark counts. Keep all framing qualitative.
