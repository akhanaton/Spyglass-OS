## What is Cambridge 9709 Pure Mathematics 1?

Cambridge 9709 is the Cambridge International AS and A Level Mathematics qualification. Pure Mathematics 1 is Paper 1 of that qualification, tested as either Paper 12 or Paper 13 depending on which variant your centre is assigned.

### Paper format and exam structure

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Paper codes | 9709/12, 9709/13 |
| Marks | 75 marks |
| Duration | 1 hour 50 minutes |
| Calculator | Scientific calculator permitted |
| Question types | Structured questions only (no multiple choice) |
| Formula sheet | Not provided -- students must know all formulae |
| Exam sessions | May/June and October/November |

Paper 1 contributes to the AS Level award (taken alone) and is also one of three papers for the full A Level. For the complete A Level, you also sit Paper 3 (Pure Mathematics 3) and one Applied paper (either Paper 4 Mechanics or Paper 5 Probability and Statistics).

### Papers 12 and 13: what's the difference?

Nothing substantial. Both papers test the same Pure Mathematics 1 syllabus at equivalent difficulty. Cambridge sets multiple variants so that students at different centres cannot share paper content. Your school is assigned one variant; you won't know which until the exam.

For revision purposes, this means you should practise both 9709/12 and 9709/13 past papers -- they're different questions on the same content, which doubles your practice material without duplicating your learning.

## Cambridge 9709 Pure 1: Complete Topic Breakdown

The [Cambridge 9709 syllabus](https://www.cambridgeinternational.org/programmes-and-qualifications/cambridge-international-as-and-a-level-mathematics-9709/) defines eight topic areas for Pure Mathematics 1. Here's what each one covers and what it looks like in the exam.

### Topic 1.1 -- Quadratics

Quadratics underpin more of the paper than students expect. The algebra here -- completing the square, using the discriminant, solving simultaneous equations -- comes back in functions, coordinate geometry, and calculus questions.

**In scope:**
- Completing the square: y = a(x + p)² + q
- Using the discriminant (b² - 4ac) to determine the nature of roots
- Solving quadratic equations and inequalities
- Simultaneous equations where one equation is linear and one is quadratic

**Exam pattern:** Quadratics rarely appear as a standalone question. More often, you'll see the discriminant used inside a coordinate geometry question ("find the range of k for which the line y = kx + 3 does not intersect the curve") or completing the square inside a functions question. Build this topic first because it's embedded everywhere else.

<!-- calloutBlock: variant="exam-tip" -->
<!-- title: Discriminant conditions to memorise -->
<!-- body: b² - 4ac > 0: two distinct real roots. b² - 4ac = 0: one repeated root (tangent to x-axis). b² - 4ac < 0: no real roots. These three conditions appear in Paper 1 questions several times per session. -->

### Topic 1.2 -- Functions

Functions introduces the formal language of A-Level algebra: domain, range, mapping, composition, and inverse. It's more conceptual than other topics -- the algebra is not especially hard, but the terminology catches students out.

**In scope:**
- Domain and range (including restricted domains)
- One-to-one functions and many-to-one functions
- Composite functions: fg(x), f²(x)
- Inverse functions: finding f⁻¹(x), domain restrictions
- Graph transformations: translations, stretches, reflections

**Exam pattern:** A typical functions question has three parts -- finding fg(x), finding f⁻¹(x), and sketching a graph. Each part is linked: if you get the composite wrong, the inverse is also wrong. Work carefully through part (i) before moving on.

For the complete topic guide, see our [A Level Maths Functions: Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/functions/).

### Topic 1.3 -- Coordinate Geometry

Coordinate geometry is one of the most mark-reliable topics on Paper 1. The questions are predictable: straight lines, circles, intersections, tangents, and normals. Students who know the method almost always score full marks.

**In scope:**
- Gradient, midpoint, distance between two points
- Equation of a straight line (y - y₁ = m(x - x₁))
- Parallel and perpendicular lines (m₁ × m₂ = -1)
- Circles: (x - a)² + (y - b)² = r²
- Intersection of lines and curves
- Tangents and normals to curves (using differentiation for gradient)

**Exam pattern:** Expect at least one coordinate geometry question per paper, often 8-10 marks. Circle questions frequently combine with the discriminant: "show that the line is a tangent to the circle" requires setting the system equal and proving b² - 4ac = 0. This is a set-piece question that appears regularly.

### Topic 1.4 -- Circular Measure

Circular measure introduces radians -- the unit that makes calculus work cleanly. Most students can convert between degrees and radians, but the application questions (arc length, sector area, segment area) cause more problems.

**In scope:**
- Radian measure and conversion: degrees ↔ radians
- Arc length: s = rθ
- Area of a sector: A = ½r²θ
- Area of a triangle and segment (combined problems)

**Exam pattern:** Circular measure questions are typically 5-7 marks and often involve a diagram. A common setup: a sector OAB with a chord AB, find the perimeter of the minor segment. You need arc length (rθ) plus chord length (from the triangle) -- students who rush miss the chord component and lose 2-3 marks.

<!-- calloutBlock: variant="common-mistake" -->
<!-- title: The perimeter trap -->
<!-- body: Perimeter of a segment = arc length + chord length. Students frequently give arc length only. The chord requires either the cosine rule or 2r sin(θ/2). Draw the diagram and label every boundary before calculating anything. -->

### Topic 1.5 -- Trigonometry

Trigonometry in Pure 1 focuses on graphs, exact values, identities, and solving equations. The key skill is finding all solutions in a given interval -- and this is where most marks are lost.

**In scope:**
- Graphs of sin x, cos x, tan x for any angle size (degrees and radians)
- Exact values at 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°
- The identities: sin²θ + cos²θ ≡ 1 and tan θ ≡ sin θ / cos θ
- Solving trigonometric equations in a given interval

**Exam pattern:** Trig equations in Paper 1 almost always require multiple solutions. "Find all values of θ in the interval 0° ≤ θ ≤ 360°" is standard -- and finding just one solution scores partial marks at best.

For exact values, worked examples, and the common mistakes examiners flag every session, see our [A Level Maths Trigonometry: Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/trigonometry/).

### Topic 1.6 -- Series

Series covers two main concepts: the binomial expansion and sequences (arithmetic and geometric). These are distinct enough that they're often taught separately, but both appear on Paper 1.

**In scope:**
- Binomial expansion: (a + b)ⁿ for positive integer n
- Binomial expansion: approximations and specific term finding
- Arithmetic progressions: nth term, sum to n terms
- Geometric progressions: nth term, sum to n terms, sum to infinity (|r| < 1)
- Conditions for convergence of geometric series

**Exam pattern:** Binomial questions often ask for a specific term (the x³ term in (2 + 3x)⁵) or for two terms to satisfy a condition. Series questions frequently involve simultaneous equations: two conditions about an AP or GP that you solve together. Recognising the structure is the key skill.

<!-- calloutBlock: variant="key-formula" -->
<!-- title: Series formulae you must know (not on formula sheet) -->
<!-- body: AP nth term: a + (n-1)d. AP sum: n/2(2a + (n-1)d). GP nth term: arⁿ⁻¹. GP sum: a(1-rⁿ)/(1-r). GP sum to infinity: a/(1-r) when |r| < 1. None of these are provided. Cambridge expects you to know them. -->

### Topic 1.7 -- Differentiation

Differentiation is the first half of calculus. It tells you the rate of change -- the gradient of a curve at any point. Together with integration, it accounts for 35-40% of Paper 1 marks.

**In scope:**
- Derivative from first principles (understanding the concept)
- Differentiating xⁿ: d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹
- Chain rule: differentiating composite functions like (3x + 2)⁴
- Second derivative and its meaning
- Stationary points (turning points, points of inflection)
- Increasing and decreasing functions
- Rates of change: connected rate-of-change problems

**Exam pattern:** A differentiation question typically builds from routine (find dy/dx) through applied (find stationary points, determine their nature) to extended (use dy/dx to find a related rate of change). Each part is worth marks independently -- work methodically even if an earlier part goes wrong.

For chain rule worked examples, stationary point questions, and the examiner-flagged sign error trap, see our [9709 P1 Differentiation: Exam Tips and Worked Solutions](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/differentiation/).

### Topic 1.8 -- Integration

Integration is the reverse of differentiation. It answers two questions: "what function was differentiated to give this?" and "what is the area under this curve?" The second application -- area problems -- is where most marks are concentrated.

**In scope:**
- Integration as the reverse of differentiation
- Integrating (ax + b)ⁿ for rational n (except n = -1)
- Definite integrals: evaluating between limits
- Area under a curve (using definite integrals)
- Area between a curve and a line

**Exam pattern:** Area questions are the most complex integration problems on Paper 1. They require finding intersection points (often using simultaneous equations), setting up the correct integral, and handling negative areas when a curve dips below the x-axis.

For every integration technique with worked examples, see our [A Level Maths Integration: Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/integration/).

## How to Revise for Cambridge 9709 Pure Mathematics 1

### The approach that actually works

Most students revise for 9709 Pure 1 the same way: they re-read their notes, work through a few examples, and then start grinding past papers. This approach has one fatal flaw -- it confuses familiarity with understanding.

Aisha had this problem. She spent four weeks reviewing her Pure 1 notes, covering every page, feeling increasingly confident. Then she sat a full Paper 1 practice exam under timed conditions. She scored 41 out of 75. The shock wasn't that the paper was hard -- it was that the questions all looked like things she recognised, but she couldn't start them without the notes in front of her.

The issue was passive revision. Reading creates the feeling of understanding without building the ability to retrieve and apply. The research on this is unambiguous: retrieval practice outperforms re-reading by a factor of two to three for exam performance (Roediger & Butler, 2011, *Perspectives on Psychological Science*).

A level maths revision works differently from revision for essay subjects. You can't summarise your way to understanding integration -- you have to do it. The same applies to every Pure 1 topic.

What works for 9709:
- **Practise questions without looking at your notes first.** If you can't start, that's information -- mark that technique as a gap.
- **Write out methods from memory, then check.** Active recall beats highlighting every time.
- **Use past papers as diagnosis, not just practice.** After each paper, identify which topics caused the most marks lost -- that's your revision list.

### 8-week revision plan for 9709 Pure 1

This plan assumes 5-6 hours of maths revision per week and an exam date approximately eight weeks away.

| Week | Focus | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Quadratics + Functions | Master completing the square, domain/range, composite and inverse functions |
| 2 | Coordinate Geometry | Straight lines, circles, tangent/normal problems |
| 3 | Circular Measure + Trigonometry | Radians, arc/sector, exact values, solving trig equations |
| 4 | Series | Binomial expansion, AP and GP, sum to infinity conditions |
| 5 | Differentiation | Chain rule, stationary points, connected rates of change |
| 6 | Integration | Reverse differentiation, definite integrals, area problems |
| 7 | Past papers (targeted) | One full paper per session, diagnose gaps, topic drill on weak areas |
| 8 | Past papers + gap-fill | One full paper + concentrated work on the two topics with most marks lost |

The plan is structured topic-by-topic in the first six weeks, not chronologically through the syllabus. Quadratics and functions come first because they underpin everything. Calculus comes last because you'll use it most in past papers and need it freshest in memory.

### How to use 9709 past papers effectively

Past papers are the most valuable revision resource you have -- but only if you use them correctly. Three common mistakes:

**Mistake 1: Using past papers without a mark scheme.** A mark scheme shows you where the method marks are, what the examiners accept as correct working, and how many steps Cambridge expects to see. Review every question against the mark scheme, not just the ones you got wrong.

**Mistake 2: Doing papers without timing.** An untimed paper tells you whether you know the maths. A timed paper tells you whether you can perform under exam conditions. These are different skills. Week 7 and Week 8 papers should always be done with 110 minutes on the clock.

**Mistake 3: Treating all past papers equally.** The 2024 Paper 12 is better revision than the 2014 Paper 12. The syllabus has evolved and examiner style changes over time. Prioritise papers from 2019 onwards -- they reflect the current examination style more closely.

## 9709 Common Mistakes: What Cambridge Examiners Flag Every Session

Cambridge publishes a Principal Examiner's Report after every examination session. These reports are one of the most underused revision resources available -- they tell you exactly what went wrong across thousands of scripts, written by the people who set and marked the paper.

Here are the mistakes that appear repeatedly, across sessions, across years.

### 1. Insufficient working shown

This is the single most cited issue in Cambridge 9709 examiner reports. Students -- particularly those using calculators -- write only the final answer. The mark scheme awards method marks for intermediate steps. No working means method marks go to zero even when the answer is right.

**The rule:** Every non-trivial algebraic step gets its own line. Every integral or derivative is shown in full. Substituting limits is shown explicitly. "Show that" questions require every step to be visible.

### 2. Losing solutions in trigonometric equations

When solving sin θ = 0.5 in 0° ≤ θ ≤ 360°, there are two solutions: 30° and 150°. Examiner reports consistently note that students find the principal value (30°) and stop, scoring half marks or less. The CAST diagram or the symmetry of the sin graph must be used to find every solution in the given interval.

**The rule:** After finding one solution, always ask: "how many solutions exist in this interval?" Sketch the graph if you're unsure.

### 3. Sign errors in differentiation and integration

Differentiating negative powers of x generates negative coefficients. Integrating produces +C. Evaluating definite integrals requires careful subtraction at the limits. Examiner reports note sign errors as a consistent source of lost marks -- particularly in chains of working where one sign error cascades through subsequent steps.

**The rule:** Write out each step fully. Don't combine two algebra moves into one line under time pressure. A single line error in a 10-step chain loses every mark that follows.

### 4. Discriminant misuse in coordinate geometry

A common Paper 1 question asks students to "show that a line is a tangent to a curve" or "find the values of k for which a line intersects a curve at two distinct points." Both require substituting the line into the curve equation and applying the discriminant. Examiner reports note students who set up the equation correctly but then apply the discriminant condition wrong -- using > 0 when = 0 is needed (tangent condition) or vice versa.

**The rule:** Tangent (one point of contact) requires b² - 4ac = 0. Two distinct intersections require b² - 4ac > 0. No real intersections require b² - 4ac < 0. Memorise all three.

### 5. Forgetting the constant of integration

In indefinite integration, +C is not optional. Cambridge examiners deduct a mark for missing constants of integration, even when every other step is correct. This applies equally to finding a general solution from a differential equation and to finding an equation of a curve from its gradient function.

**The rule:** Write +C immediately after integrating, before you do anything else.
