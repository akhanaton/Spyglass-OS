# A Level Maths Trigonometry: Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Revision Guide

> *This guide is part of our [Complete Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Revision Guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/), your comprehensive resource for exam preparation.*

A level maths trigonometry isn't about memorising formulas. It's about knowing which tool to reach for when the equation in front of you doesn't look like the one in the textbook.

The students who score full marks on 9709 trigonometry questions have a system: a method for finding every solution, a strategy for proving identities, and exact values they can derive in seconds rather than recall under pressure. The students who lose marks? They find one solution and stop. They divide by sin θ and lose half their answers. They freeze when an identity proof doesn't start the way they expected.

This guide gives you the system. It covers everything in Topic 1.5 of the Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 syllabus, including the common mistakes that Cambridge examiners flag year after year. Whether you're sitting Paper 11, 12, or 13, the trigonometry content is identical across all three variants.

## What Cambridge 9709 Trigonometry Actually Covers

Topic 1.5 in the [9709 Pure Mathematics 1 syllabus](https://www.cambridgeinternational.org/programmes-and-qualifications/cambridge-international-as-and-a-level-mathematics-9709/) is more focused than most students expect. Here's exactly what's in scope:

- Graphs of sin, cos, and tan for angles of any size (degrees and radians)
- Exact values of sin, cos, and tan at 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°
- The identity sin²θ + cos²θ ≡ 1
- The identity tan θ ≡ sin θ / cos θ
- Solving trigonometric equations in a given interval
- Inverse trig notation: sin⁻¹x (arcsin), cos⁻¹x (arccos), tan⁻¹x (arctan)

What's **not** in Pure 1: compound angle formulae (sin(A+B), cos(A+B)), double angle formulae, sec, cosec, cot, and R-formulae. All of those are Pure 3. Don't revise them for Paper 1.

Trigonometry typically accounts for 10-15 marks directly on a 75-mark paper. But trig values also appear inside coordinate geometry and graph sketching questions, pushing the true contribution closer to 15-20 marks. That's roughly a quarter of your Paper 1 grade hinging on one topic.

## Exact Values in A Level Maths Trigonometry (And How to Never Forget Them)

Most exact values trigonometry a level resources tell you to memorise the table. We're going to do something better: derive the entire table from two triangles in about 30 seconds.

### The Two Triangles That Give You Everything

**Triangle 1: The 30-60-90 triangle.** Take an equilateral triangle with side length 2. Cut it in half down the middle. You now have a right-angled triangle with:
- Hypotenuse = 2 (the original side)
- Short side = 1 (half the base)
- Long side = √3 (by Pythagoras: √(4 - 1))
- Angles: 30°, 60°, 90°

From this single triangle, read off using SOH CAH TOA:
- sin 30° = 1/2, cos 30° = √3/2, tan 30° = 1/√3
- sin 60° = √3/2, cos 60° = 1/2, tan 60° = √3

**Triangle 2: The 45-45-90 triangle.** Take an isosceles right-angled triangle with equal sides of length 1. The hypotenuse = √2. That gives you:
- sin 45° = 1/√2, cos 45° = 1/√2, tan 45° = 1

[IMAGE: trig-exact-values-triangles.svg, The two triangles that give you every exact value; derive, don't memorise]

### The Complete Exact Values Table

| Angle (°) | Angle (rad) | sin θ | cos θ | tan θ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0° | 0 | 0 | 1 | 0 |
| 30° | π/6 | 1/2 | √3/2 | 1/√3 |
| 45° | π/4 | 1/√2 | 1/√2 | 1 |
| 60° | π/3 | √3/2 | 1/2 | √3 |
| 90° | π/2 | 1 | 0 | undefined |

Notice the pattern: the sin column reads 0, 1/2, 1/√2, √3/2, 1. The cos column is the same values in reverse order. Once you see that, the table writes itself.

### Why This Matters in the Exam

When a 9709 question says "leave your answer in exact form," decimals score zero. You need sin 60° = √3/2, not 0.866. The two-triangle derivation takes less time than trying to recall a memorised table under pressure, and it's impossible to get wrong if you know Pythagoras.

> **Exam tip:** Write the two triangles in the margin at the start of the exam. It takes 10 seconds and gives you a reference for the entire paper.

## Trigonometric Graphs and Transformations

### The Shape of Sin, Cos, and Tan

The sin cos tan graphs a level students need to sketch from memory:

**y = sin x**: Starts at 0, rises to 1 at π/2, returns to 0 at π, falls to -1 at 3π/2, completes one cycle at 2π. Amplitude 1, period 2π.

**y = cos x**: Starts at 1, falls to 0 at π/2, reaches -1 at π, returns to 0 at 3π/2, completes at 2π. Same shape as sin x, shifted left by π/2. Amplitude 1, period 2π.

**y = tan x** (tangent): Passes through the origin, has vertical asymptotes at π/2, 3π/2, etc. No amplitude (the range is all real numbers). Period π, not 2π.

### Transformations of Trig Graphs

The function y = a sin(bx + c) + d transforms y = sin x in four ways. The rules are the same as the [graph transformations in the functions topic](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/functions/), applied to trig curves:

| Parameter | Effect | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| a | Vertical stretch, scale factor a | As expected |
| b | Horizontal stretch, scale factor 1/b (changes period to 2π/b) | Opposite |
| c | Horizontal shift (phase shift) by c/b | Opposite |
| d | Vertical shift up by d | As expected |

**The order matters.** Apply transformations inside the function first (horizontal), then outside (vertical). Getting the order wrong produces a different curve.

**Worked example: Sketch y = 2cos(x - π/3) + 1**

Step 1: Start with y = cos x.
Step 2: Replace x with (x - π/3). This shifts the curve right by π/3.
Step 3: Multiply by 2. This stretches vertically by factor 2 (amplitude becomes 2).
Step 4: Add 1. This shifts everything up by 1 (the centre line moves from y = 0 to y = 1).

The result oscillates between -1 and 3, with period 2π, shifted right by π/3.

[IMAGE: trig-graph-transformation-steps.svg, Building y = 2cos(x - π/3) + 1 from y = cos x, one transformation at a time]

> **Remember:** Check your calculator mode before you start any trig question. Radians or degrees? If the domain is 0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π, you need radians. If it's 0° ≤ θ ≤ 360°, you need degrees. Wrong calculator mode gives completely wrong answers and costs all the marks.

## Solving Trigonometric Equations at A Level

Solving trigonometric equations a level students face in 9709 is where most marks are won and lost. The [Cambridge examiner reports](https://www.cambridgeinternational.org/programmes-and-qualifications/cambridge-international-as-and-a-level-mathematics-9709/) flag the same errors every year: students find one solution when three exist, or they divide by a trig function and lose half their answers.

### The 3-Step Method That Works Every Time

1. **Get to a single trig function.** If the equation has both sin and cos, use an identity to convert. If it's already a single function (sin θ = 0.5), skip to Step 2.
2. **Find the principal value.** Use your calculator (inverse sin, cos, or tan) or exact values to find the first solution.
3. **Find ALL solutions in the domain.** This is the step students skip. Use the CAST diagram or symmetry properties to identify every solution within the given interval.

[IMAGE: trig-cast-diagram.svg, CAST diagram: All, Sin, Tan, Cos; which functions are positive in each quadrant]

**The CAST diagram** tells you which trig functions are positive in each quadrant. (Note: Pure 1 asks for solutions in a given interval, not the general solution — that's Pure 3.) Reading anticlockwise from the fourth quadrant: Cos, All, Sin, Tan. If sin θ = 0.5, sin is positive in Q1 and Q2, giving you θ = π/6 and θ = π - π/6 = 5π/6.

### When It's a Quadratic in Disguise

Some trig equations look complicated but are actually quadratics. The giveaway: you see sin²θ or cos²θ alongside a linear sin θ or cos θ term.

**Example:** 2sin²θ - sin θ - 1 = 0

This is a quadratic in sin θ. Let u = sin θ:
- 2u² - u - 1 = 0
- (2u + 1)(u - 1) = 0
- u = -1/2 or u = 1
- sin θ = -1/2 or sin θ = 1

Now find all angles in the given domain for each value. Always check validity: sin θ can only be between -1 and 1, so reject any solutions outside that range.

> **Examiner insight:** Some candidates try to apply trig identities to quadratic equations instead of recognising the quadratic structure. Look at the shape of the equation first. If it looks like ax² + bx + c = 0 with a trig function in place of x, factorise it.

### Using the Identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

When an equation contains both sin and cos (and at least one is squared), use sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to convert everything to a single function.

**Example:** 2cos²θ + sin θ = 1

Replace cos²θ with 1 - sin²θ:
- 2(1 - sin²θ) + sin θ = 1
- 2 - 2sin²θ + sin θ = 1
- 2sin²θ - sin θ - 1 = 0

Now it's the quadratic from above. Solve as before.

### The Biggest Trap: Losing Solutions by Dividing

This is the single most common mark-losing error in 9709 trigonometry, and examiners call it out explicitly.

**Wrong approach:** sin θ cos θ = sin θ → divide both sides by sin θ → cos θ = 1 → θ = 0° only.

**Correct approach:** sin θ cos θ - sin θ = 0 → sin θ(cos θ - 1) = 0 → sin θ = 0 OR cos θ = 1.

Dividing by sin θ threw away every solution where sin θ = 0. That's θ = 0°, 180°, 360° gone. Factorising keeps them all.

**The rule is simple: never divide both sides of a trig equation by a trig function. Factorise instead.**

## Proving Trigonometric Identities in Cambridge 9709

Proving trigonometric identities appears on most 9709 Paper 1 exams. The trigonometric identities a level exams test are surprisingly few — just two for Pure 1 — but identity proofs look intimidating until you have a system.

### The 4-Rule Approach That Gets Full Marks

1. **Start with the more complicated side.** Pick the side with more terms, fractions, or mixed functions. Work that side until it matches the other.
2. **Convert everything to sin and cos.** Replace tan θ with sin θ / cos θ. This gives you a common language to work with.
3. **Look for sin²θ + cos²θ = 1.** After combining fractions or expanding, this identity usually appears. It's the key simplification step in most Pure 1 proofs.
4. **Work down one side only.** Never write LHS = RHS as your starting line. That assumes what you're trying to prove. Start with one side and transform it step by step until it equals the other side.

### Worked Example: Prove That (sin θ)/(1 + cos θ) + (1 + cos θ)/(sin θ) ≡ 2/sin θ

Start with the LHS (more complicated):

$$\frac{\sin\theta}{1+\cos\theta} + \frac{1+\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}$$

Combine fractions (common denominator = sin θ(1 + cos θ)):

$$= \frac{\sin^2\theta + (1+\cos\theta)^2}{\sin\theta(1+\cos\theta)}$$

Expand the numerator:

$$= \frac{\sin^2\theta + 1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta(1+\cos\theta)}$$

Apply sin²θ + cos²θ = 1:

$$= \frac{1 + 1 + 2\cos\theta}{\sin\theta(1+\cos\theta)} = \frac{2 + 2\cos\theta}{\sin\theta(1+\cos\theta)}$$

Factor the numerator:

$$= \frac{2(1 + \cos\theta)}{\sin\theta(1+\cos\theta)} = \frac{2}{\sin\theta} \quad \text{QED}$$

Notice how every step follows the rules: we started with the complicated side, combined fractions, expanded, applied the Pythagorean identity, and factored. The same sequence works for nearly every Pure 1 identity proof.

## 5 Trigonometry Mistakes That Cost Marks Every Year

These come directly from Cambridge 9709 examiner reports (2019-2025). Every one of them is avoidable.

**Mistake 1: Finding only one solution.** sin θ = 0.5 has two solutions in [0, 2π], not one. After finding the principal value (π/6), use CAST or symmetry to find the second (5π/6). Examiner reports flag "only one solution found" as the most frequent trig error.

**Mistake 2: Dividing by sin θ or cos θ.** This throws away solutions. Always rearrange and factorise instead. See the worked example above.

**Mistake 3: Using the wrong identity.** Under exam pressure, students write sin θ = 1 - cos θ instead of sin²θ + cos²θ = 1. The identity involves squares. Write it correctly on your formula sheet before the exam starts.

**Mistake 4: Calculator in the wrong mode.** If the domain is in radians (0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π) and your calculator is in degrees, every answer will be wrong. Check the mode before you start. Then check it again.

**Mistake 5: Not showing working.** Writing final answers from a calculator without method steps loses method marks, even if the answers are correct. Examiners need to see: the equation you solved, the principal value, how you found additional solutions, and confirmation that solutions are within the domain. Check the mark scheme for any past paper question and you'll see that method marks outnumber answer marks in every trig question.

## How Trigonometry Connects to Other 9709 Topics

A level maths trigonometry doesn't exist in isolation. The exact values feed into coordinate geometry questions involving gradients at specific angles. The graph transformations use the same rules you learnt in the [functions topic](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/functions/), applied to sine and cosine curves instead of algebraic functions.

Radians come from circular measure (Topic 1.4) in the [Pure 1 syllabus](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/). If you're shaky on converting between degrees and radians, fix that before tackling trig equations in radians.

And while you won't differentiate or integrate trig functions in Pure 1, understanding trigonometric graphs helps you visualise rates of change when you reach [differentiation](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/differentiation/) and the area calculations in [integration](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/integration/). The gradient of sin x at x = 0 is 1 (steep upward slope), and at x = π/2 it's 0 (the peak). That visual intuition pays off.

## Practice Questions With Worked Solutions (9709 Exam Style)

**Question 1 (Easy, 2 marks):** Find the exact value of sin 60° + cos 30°.

**Solution:** Both come from the 30-60-90 triangle. sin 60° = √3/2 and cos 30° = √3/2. So sin 60° + cos 30° = √3/2 + √3/2 = √3.

---

**Question 2 (Easy, 3 marks):** Solve sin θ = 0.5 for 0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π.

**Solution:** Principal value: sin⁻¹(0.5) = π/6. Sin is positive in Q1 and Q2. Second solution: π - π/6 = 5π/6. Solutions: θ = π/6 and θ = 5π/6.

---

**Question 3 (Medium, 5 marks):** Solve 2sin²θ - sin θ - 1 = 0 for 0° ≤ θ ≤ 360°.

**Solution:** Let u = sin θ. Then 2u² - u - 1 = 0 → (2u + 1)(u - 1) = 0 → u = -1/2 or u = 1. For sin θ = 1: θ = 90°. For sin θ = -1/2: sin is negative in Q3 and Q4. Principal value for sin⁻¹(1/2) = 30°. So θ = 180° + 30° = 210° and θ = 360° - 30° = 330°. Solutions: θ = 90°, 210°, 330°.

---

**Question 4 (Medium, 4 marks):** Prove that (sin θ)/(1 + cos θ) + (1 + cos θ)/(sin θ) ≡ 2/sin θ.

**Solution:** See the worked identity proof in the section above.

---

**Question 5 (Hard, 6 marks):** The function y = 3sin(2x - π/4) + 1 is defined for 0 ≤ x ≤ 2π. State the amplitude and period. Find the x-coordinate of the first maximum.

**Solution:** Amplitude = 3 (the coefficient of sin). Period = 2π/2 = π (since b = 2). Maximum occurs when the argument equals π/2: 2x - π/4 = π/2 → 2x = 3π/4 → x = 3π/8.

## FAQs: Cambridge 9709 Trigonometry

**What trigonometric identities do I need for 9709 Pure 1?**

The trig identities a level maths students need for Pure 1 are just two: sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 and tan θ = sin θ / cos θ. Compound angle formulae (sin(A+B), double angle formulae, R-formulae) are all Pure 3. Don't revise them for Paper 1.

**Is trigonometry harder than differentiation in 9709?**

Different challenge. Differentiation has clear rules you apply mechanically. A level maths trigonometry requires pattern recognition: spotting which method to use and remembering to find all solutions. Students who have a systematic approach find trig straightforward. Students who wing it lose marks.

**Do I need compound angle formulae for Pure 1?**

No. sin(A+B), cos(A+B), tan(A+B), double angle formulae, and R-formulae are all Pure Mathematics 3 content. Pure 1 uses only exact values, graphs, transformations, the Pythagorean identity, and tan θ = sin θ / cos θ.

**Should I use radians or degrees?**

Use whatever the question specifies. If the domain is 0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π, work in radians. If it's 0° ≤ θ ≤ 360°, work in degrees. The critical thing is matching your calculator mode to the question.

**How many marks is trigonometry worth in Paper 1?**

Typically 10-15 marks directly (out of 75 total). Trig values and transformations also appear in other questions, so the true contribution is closer to 15-20 marks, roughly 20-25% of your Paper 1 grade.

A level maths trigonometry in Cambridge 9709 rewards a systematic approach over memorisation. Derive your exact values from two triangles. Use the 3-step method for every equation.
