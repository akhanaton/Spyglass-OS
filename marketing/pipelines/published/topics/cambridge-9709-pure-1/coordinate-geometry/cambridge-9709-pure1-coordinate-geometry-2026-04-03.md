# A Level Maths Coordinate Geometry: Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Guide

> *This guide is part of our [Complete Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Revision Guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/), your comprehensive resource for exam preparation.*

A level maths coordinate geometry covers the properties and equations of straight lines and circles on the Cartesian plane. In Cambridge 9709 Pure 1, it appears as Topic 1.3 and typically accounts for 9-12 marks on Paper 1 -- making it one of the most predictable and mark-reliable topics on the exam.

A level maths coordinate geometry is the most mark-reliable topic on 9709 Paper 1. The question types are predictable, the methods are consistent, and students who know the system score full marks. The students who don't? They lose marks on sign errors in circle equations, forget the tangent-radius relationship, and never draw the diagram that would have shown them the answer.

This guide covers everything in Topic 1.3 of the [Cambridge 9709 Pure Mathematics 1 syllabus](https://www.cambridgeinternational.org/programmes-and-qualifications/cambridge-international-as-and-a-level-mathematics-9709/), including the common mistakes that Cambridge examiners flag year after year. Topic 1.3 in the 9709 Pure 1 syllabus is tightly scoped. Here's exactly what's in and what's out:

## Straight Lines: The Foundation

### Gradient, Midpoint, and Distance

The midpoint distance formula a level students need sits at the core of every straight-line question. These three formulae underpin everything. You need them cold.

**Gradient** between two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$:

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

**Midpoint** of a line segment:

$$M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\ \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$$

**Distance** between two points:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$$

When a question says "leave your answer in exact form," keep surds as surds. Priya finds the distance between $(1, 3)$ and $(4, 7)$ as $\sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$. Clean. But when the answer is $\sqrt{52}$, she simplifies to $2\sqrt{13}$ rather than reaching for the calculator. That's the habit that earns full marks.

### Equation of a Straight Line

The equation of a straight line a level questions test three forms, and knowing when to use each saves time:

**Slope-intercept form:** $y = mx + c$
Use when you already know the gradient and the y-intercept. Read $m$ and $c$ directly.

**Point-slope form:** $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$
Use when you know the gradient and any point on the line. This is your workhorse form -- it handles most exam questions.

**General form:** $ax + by + c = 0$
Use when the question asks for integer coefficients or when working with parallel and perpendicular conditions.

<!-- calloutBlock: variant="warning" -->

**Common trap:** If the equation is $2y = 6x + 4$, the gradient is not 6. Rearrange to $y = 3x + 2$ first. The gradient is 3. Examiners see this error constantly.

<!-- /calloutBlock -->

### Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Perpendicular lines a level maths questions rely on two conditions to know:

**Parallel lines** have equal gradients: $m_1 = m_2$

**Perpendicular lines** satisfy: $m_1 \times m_2 = -1$

The perpendicular gradient is the negative reciprocal. If $m_1 = 3$, then $m_2 = -\frac{1}{3}$. If $m_1 = -\frac{2}{5}$, then $m_2 = \frac{5}{2}$.

**Worked example: Find the perpendicular bisector of the line segment from $A(2, 1)$ to $B(6, 5)$.**

Step 1: Find the midpoint. $M = \left(\frac{2+6}{2},\ \frac{1+5}{2}\right) = (4, 3)$

Step 2: Find the gradient of $AB$. $m_{AB} = \frac{5-1}{6-2} = \frac{4}{4} = 1$

Step 3: Find the perpendicular gradient. $m_\perp = -\frac{1}{1} = -1$

Step 4: Use point-slope form with $M(4, 3)$. $y - 3 = -1(x - 4) \Rightarrow y = -x + 7$

This is a four-step chain. One error in any step cascades through the rest. Write each step clearly -- method marks save you if the final answer is wrong.

## Circles: The Topic That Catches Students Out

### The Circle Equation

Two forms, and you must convert fluently between them.

**Standard form** (centre-radius form):

$$(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2$$

Centre $(a, b)$, radius $r$. Read the values directly.

**General form:**

$$x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0$$

Centre $(-g, -f)$, radius $r = \sqrt{g^2 + f^2 - c}$

Converting from general to standard form requires completing the square for the circle equation -- and this is where circle equations a level maths questions go wrong most often.

**Worked example: Find the centre and radius of $x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0$.**

Group the $x$ and $y$ terms:

$$(x^2 - 6x) + (y^2 + 4y) = 12$$

Complete the square for $x$: $x^2 - 6x = (x - 3)^2 - 9$

Complete the square for $y$: $y^2 + 4y = (y + 2)^2 - 4$

Substitute back:

$$(x - 3)^2 - 9 + (y + 2)^2 - 4 = 12$$

$$(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$$

Centre $(3, -2)$, radius $5$.

<!-- calloutBlock: variant="mistake" -->

**Sign trap:** In the equation $(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$, the centre is $(3, -2)$, not $(3, 2)$. The signs flip. In the general form $x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0$, the centre is $(-g, -f)$, not $(g, f)$. [Cambridge examiner reports](https://www.cambridgeinternational.org/programmes-and-qualifications/cambridge-international-as-and-a-level-mathematics-9709/) cite this as the most common coordinate geometry error across every exam session.

<!-- /calloutBlock -->

### Tangents and Normals to Circles

The key principle: a tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of contact. Every tangent to a circle a level question depends on this single fact. Understanding how to find the equation of a tangent to a circle a level students face regularly is essential for full marks.

**Method for finding the tangent at a point on a circle:**

Step 1: Find the gradient of the radius from the centre to the point.

Step 2: Take the negative reciprocal to get the tangent gradient.

Step 3: Use $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ with the tangent gradient and the contact point.

**Worked example:** Find the equation of the tangent to the circle $(x - 2)^2 + (y - 3)^2 = 20$ at the point $(6, 5)$.

Step 1: Centre is $(2, 3)$. Gradient of radius to $(6, 5)$:

$$m_r = \frac{5 - 3}{6 - 2} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$$

Step 2: Tangent gradient: $m_t = -\frac{1}{m_r} = -2$

Step 3: $y - 5 = -2(x - 6) \Rightarrow y = -2x + 17$

Omar gets to Step 2 and writes $m_t = \frac{1}{2}$ instead of $-2$. He used the radius gradient directly, forgetting the negative reciprocal. This costs three marks minimum. The fix is simple: always write "perpendicular $\Rightarrow$ negative reciprocal" in your working.

### Line-Circle Intersection Problems

This is the set-piece question type that appears almost every session. The method:

1. Substitute the line equation into the circle equation
2. Expand and simplify to get a quadratic
3. Apply the discriminant:
 - $b^2 - 4ac > 0$ -- two intersection points
 - $b^2 - 4ac = 0$ -- tangent (one repeated root)
 - $b^2 - 4ac < 0$ -- no intersection

The discriminant connects directly to quadratics (Topic 1.1). If your discriminant work is shaky, review our [9709 quadratics guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/quadratics/) and fix that first.

**"Show that the line is a tangent to the circle"** is the most common exam phrasing. Substitute, form the quadratic, and show $b^2 - 4ac = 0$.

## Intersection of Lines and Curves

Beyond circles, 9709 questions ask you to find where a straight line meets a parabola or other curve.

**Method:** Substitute the linear equation into the curve equation and solve the resulting equation simultaneously.

Fatima is asked: "Find the values of $k$ for which the line $y = 2x + k$ does not intersect the curve $y = x^2 + 3x + 5$."

She sets $2x + k = x^2 + 3x + 5$, rearranges to $x^2 + x + (5 - k) = 0$, and applies the "no intersection" condition: $b^2 - 4ac < 0$.

$$1 - 4(1)(5 - k) < 0$$
$$1 - 20 + 4k < 0$$
$$4k < 19$$
$$k < \frac{19}{4}$$

This is where 9709 pure 1 coordinate geometry meets the discriminant from quadratics -- both must be secure. See our [9709 Integration Guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/integration/) for area methods that extend these intersection ideas to finding areas between curves and lines.

## 5 A Level Coordinate Geometry Mistakes That Cost Marks Every Year

These come directly from Cambridge 9709 examiner reports (2022-2025). Every a level maths coordinate geometry mistake below is avoidable.

**Mistake 1: Sign errors when completing the square for circles.** Students write the centre of $(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$ as $(3, 2)$ instead of $(3, -2)$. The sign in the bracket is the opposite of the coordinate. Write it out explicitly every time.

**Mistake 2: Forgetting the tangent-radius perpendicular relationship.** Finding the gradient of the radius is only half the job. The tangent gradient is the negative reciprocal. Write "perp $\Rightarrow$ neg recip" in your working as a forcing function.

**Mistake 3: Not rearranging before reading the gradient.** If the equation is $3x + 2y = 8$, the gradient is not 3. Rearrange to $y = -\frac{3}{2}x + 4$. The gradient is $-\frac{3}{2}$.

**Mistake 4: Rounding surds prematurely.** When the distance is $\sqrt{52}$, leave it as $2\sqrt{13}$. Don't write 7.21. Exact form means exact form. Premature rounding loses accuracy marks and sometimes method marks too.

**Mistake 5: Not drawing a diagram.** The geometry often reveals the method faster than algebra alone. A quick sketch showing the circle, the point, and the tangent makes the perpendicular relationship obvious. Examiners note that candidates who draw diagrams consistently produce more accurate work.

## FAQs: Cambridge 9709 Coordinate Geometry

**How many marks is coordinate geometry worth in 9709 Paper 1?**

Typically 9-12 marks directly out of 75 total. When combined with differentiation-based tangent and normal questions, the true contribution is closer to 15-18 marks -- roughly 20-24% of your Paper 1 grade.

**Do I need to know the equation of a circle for Pure 1?**

Yes. Circle equations are part of Topic 1.3 in the 9709 syllabus. You need both the standard form $(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2$ and the general form $x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0$, plus the ability to convert between them.

**What's the difference between a tangent and a normal?**

A tangent touches the circle (or curve) at exactly one point and is perpendicular to the radius at that point. A normal passes through the same point but runs along the radius direction -- it's perpendicular to the tangent.

**How do I "show that a line is a tangent to a circle"?**

Substitute the line equation into the circle equation. Expand and simplify to get a quadratic. Show that the discriminant $b^2 - 4ac = 0$. This proves the line meets the circle at exactly one point, which is the definition of a tangent.

**Should I always draw a diagram?**

Yes. Even a rough sketch helps you see the geometric relationships that make the algebra simpler. Examiners consistently note that candidates who draw diagrams make fewer errors and choose more efficient methods.
