# A Level Maths Quadratics: Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Guide

> *This guide is part of our [Complete Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Revision Guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/), your comprehensive resource for exam preparation.*

You can already solve a level maths quadratics. Factorising, the quadratic formula, sketching parabolas -- you've covered all of that at GCSE. So what makes the Cambridge 9709 version different?

The short answer: 9709 doesn't test whether you can solve quadratics. It tests whether you can use quadratic techniques as tools inside other problems. The discriminant proves a line is tangent to a curve. Completing the square converts a circle equation into centre-radius form. A trigonometry question that looks nothing like a quadratic turns out to be one in disguise.

Quadratics is the foundational topic in Pure 1. Get it right and you'll unlock marks across half the paper. Get it wrong and the errors cascade through coordinate geometry, functions, trigonometry, and differentiation.

This guide covers everything in Topic 1.1 of the [Cambridge 9709 Pure Mathematics 1 syllabus](https://www.cambridgeinternational.org/programmes-and-qualifications/cambridge-international-as-and-a-level-mathematics-9709/), with a focus on the techniques and mistakes that Cambridge examiners flag year after year.

## Completing the Square -- The Skill That Unlocks Everything

### The Standard Method

Completing the square a level questions demand speed and accuracy. The method converts $ax^2 + bx + c$ into vertex form $a(x + p)^2 + q$, which gives you the turning point directly.

For a monic quadratic (leading coefficient 1):

$$x^2 + bx + c = \left(x + \frac{b}{2}\right)^2 + c - \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2$$

**Worked example:** Complete the square for $x^2 + 6x + 2$.

Half the coefficient of $x$: $6 \div 2 = 3$. Square it: $3^2 = 9$. So:

$$x^2 + 6x + 2 = (x + 3)^2 + 2 - 9 = (x + 3)^2 - 7$$

The vertex is at $(-3, -7)$. No calculus needed.

For a non-monic quadratic, factor out the leading coefficient first:

$$2x^2 + 12x + 5 = 2(x^2 + 6x) + 5 = 2\left[(x + 3)^2 - 9\right] + 5 = 2(x + 3)^2 - 13$$

### Completing the Square with Negative Leading Coefficients

This is where marks get lost. Cambridge examiner reports cite sign errors with negative leading coefficients every session.

**Worked example:** Express $-x^2 + 6x - 5$ in the form $a(x + p)^2 + q$.

Priya is working through this question. She factors out $-1$ from the $x$ terms:

$$-x^2 + 6x - 5 = -(x^2 - 6x) - 5$$

Now she completes the square inside the bracket:

$$= -\left[(x - 3)^2 - 9\right] - 5$$

Distribute the negative sign carefully:

$$= -(x - 3)^2 + 9 - 5 = -(x - 3)^2 + 4$$

The vertex is at $(3, 4)$ and the parabola opens downward. The most common error is writing $-9 - 5$ instead of $+9 - 5$ when distributing the negative sign. If you get a vertex that doesn't make sense when you sketch the parabola, go back and check your sign distribution.

### How Completing the Square Connects to Circles

The same technique converts the general equation of a circle into centre-radius form. When you see $x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0$ in your [coordinate geometry](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/coordinate-geometry/) questions, you complete the square on both $x$ and $y$ separately to find the centre $(-g, -f)$ and radius. If you can complete the square on a quadratic, you can handle circle equations. They are the same skill.

## The Discriminant -- Your Multi-Purpose Tool

### The Three Conditions

The discriminant a level maths students need is $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ from the quadratic $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$. Three conditions, three outcomes:

| Condition | Meaning | Geometric interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| $b^2 - 4ac > 0$ | Two distinct real roots | Parabola crosses the $x$-axis twice |
| $b^2 - 4ac = 0$ | One repeated root | Parabola touches the $x$-axis (tangent) |
| $b^2 - 4ac < 0$ | No real roots | Parabola does not meet the $x$-axis |

The table itself is straightforward. The difficulty isn't memorising it. It's knowing when to use each condition.

### Where the Discriminant Appears in 9709

The discriminant isn't just a quadratics tool. It appears across at least four other topics:

**Coordinate geometry:** "Show that the line $y = 2x + k$ is a tangent to the curve $y = x^2 + 3$." Substitute the linear equation into the quadratic, rearrange, and set $b^2 - 4ac = 0$ because a tangent touches at exactly one point. This type of question appears in the [coordinate geometry](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/coordinate-geometry/) section almost every session.

**Functions:** "Find the range of values of $k$ for which $f(x) = k$ has no solutions." Set $f(x) = k$, rearrange into a quadratic, and apply $b^2 - 4ac < 0$. See our [functions guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/functions/) for more on range problems.

**"Find the values of k" questions:** These are the ones students find hardest. Omar sees the question: "Find the values of $k$ for which $x^2 + kx + 9 = 0$ has equal roots." He identifies $a = 1$, $b = k$, $c = 9$, sets $b^2 - 4ac = 0$:

$$k^2 - 4(1)(9) = 0$$
$$k^2 = 36$$
$$k = \pm 6$$

The examiners want both values. Forgetting the negative root is a common slip.

> **Exam tip:** When a question says "find the values of $k$," expect two answers. When it says "find the range of values of $k$," expect an inequality.

## Hidden Quadratics -- Spotting the Disguise

### What Are Hidden Quadratics?

Hidden quadratics a level maths questions -- also known as disguised quadratics a level maths problems -- are equations that don't look like quadratics but become quadratics after a substitution. The pattern to recognise: three terms where the power of the first term is twice the power of the second.

This is the A-Level concept that GCSE didn't prepare you for. It appears in trigonometry, exponentials, and algebraic questions -- often without any hint that there's a quadratic involved.

### Common Disguises in 9709

**Trigonometric:** $\sin^2 x + 3\sin x + 2 = 0$

Let $u = \sin x$. The equation becomes $u^2 + 3u + 2 = 0$, which factorises to $(u + 1)(u + 2) = 0$. So $u = -1$ or $u = -2$. Since $-1 \leq \sin x \leq 1$, reject $u = -2$. Then $\sin x = -1$ gives $x = 270°$ (or $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ in radians). This connects directly to the equation-solving techniques in our [trigonometry guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/trigonometry/).

**Exponential:** $4^x - 6(2^x) + 8 = 0$

Notice that $4^x = (2^2)^x = (2^x)^2$. Let $u = 2^x$:

$$u^2 - 6u + 8 = 0$$
$$(u - 2)(u - 4) = 0$$

So $u = 2$ or $u = 4$. Converting back: $2^x = 2$ gives $x = 1$, and $2^x = 4$ gives $x = 2$.

**Algebraic:** $x^4 - 5x^2 + 4 = 0$

Let $u = x^2$. Then $u^2 - 5u + 4 = 0$, giving $(u - 1)(u - 4) = 0$. So $u = 1$ or $u = 4$, meaning $x = \pm 1$ or $x = \pm 2$.

### The Substitution Checklist

Use this every time you suspect a hidden quadratic:

1. Identify the "base" expression (e.g. $\sin x$, $2^x$, $x^2$)
2. Check the powers -- is the first term the square of the second?
3. Substitute $u =$ [base expression]
4. Solve the quadratic in $u$
5. Convert back to the original variable
6. Check validity -- reject solutions outside the domain

Step 6 is where Fatima lost marks on her mock exam. She solved a hidden quadratic in $\sin x$ and accepted $\sin x = 3$ as a solution. Since $\sin x$ can never exceed 1, that solution is invalid. Always check back against the domain of the original expression. If $u = 2^x$, then $u$ must be positive. If $u = \sin x$, then $-1 \leq u \leq 1$.

## Quadratic Inequalities

### Finding Critical Values

Quadratic inequalities a level questions start the same way as equations: find the roots. Solve $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$ to get $x = 2$ and $x = 3$. These are your critical values.

### The Sketch Method

Here's the reliable method for determining the solution set:

1. **Sketch the parabola.** Is the coefficient of $x^2$ positive (opens upward) or negative (opens downward)?
2. **Mark the roots on the $x$-axis.** These are the critical values you just found.
3. **Read the inequality from the graph.** If the question asks where the expression is greater than zero, shade the regions where the parabola is above the $x$-axis. If it asks where the expression is less than zero, shade below.

**Worked example:** Solve $x^2 - 5x + 6 > 0$.

The parabola $y = x^2 - 5x + 6$ opens upward (positive $x^2$ coefficient) and crosses the $x$-axis at $x = 2$ and $x = 3$. It is above the axis when $x < 2$ or $x > 3$.

Solution: $x < 2$ or $x > 3$.

For $x^2 - 5x + 6 < 0$, the parabola is below the axis between the roots: $2 < x < 3$.

### Why the Algebraic Method Can Mislead

Students who try to solve inequalities purely algebraically often make sign errors when multiplying or dividing by expressions that could be negative. The sketch method sidesteps this entirely. Factorise, find the roots, draw the parabola, and read the answer from the picture. It works every time, and examiners give full credit for a clearly labelled sketch.

## 5 Quadratic Mistakes That Cost Marks in 9709

These come from [Cambridge 9709 examiner reports](https://www.cambridgeinternational.org/programmes-and-qualifications/cambridge-international-as-and-a-level-mathematics-9709/) (2022-2025). Every one is avoidable.

**Mistake 1: Sign errors in completing the square.** Especially with negative leading coefficients. The fix: always distribute the factored-out coefficient back through before moving on, and verify by expanding your answer.

**Mistake 2: Applying the wrong discriminant condition.** Using $b^2 - 4ac > 0$ when the question requires $b^2 - 4ac = 0$ (tangent condition). Understanding the nature of roots through the discriminant is one of the most frequently tested skills. The fix: read the question for the word "tangent," "touches," or "equal roots" -- all mean $\Delta = 0$.

**Mistake 3: Not checking hidden quadratic solutions against the domain.** Accepting $\sin x = 3$ or $2^x = -1$ as valid. The fix: step 6 of the substitution checklist. Always convert back and verify.

**Mistake 4: Confusing completing the square with solving.** Students complete the square correctly but then write $x = \ldots$ when the question asked for the form $a(x + p)^2 + q$. The fix: re-read the question. "Express in the form" and "solve" are different instructions.

**Mistake 5: Solving a quadratic inequality as an equation.** Writing $x = 2$ and $x = 3$ instead of $x < 2$ or $x > 3$. Inequalities require a range, not isolated values. The fix: always sketch the parabola and read the solution as a region. Check the mark scheme for any past paper question to see how examiners award method marks for quadratic techniques -- you'll notice they require the inequality notation, not just the critical values.

## FAQs -- Cambridge 9709 Quadratics

**Is completing the square harder than the quadratic formula?**

They are different tools for different jobs. The quadratic formula finds roots. Completing the square gives you the vertex form, which you need for finding the range of a function, the turning point of a curve, and converting circle equations. You need both for 9709.

**Do I need to know the quadratic formula for 9709?**

Yes. Completing the square isn't always the quickest method, especially when the coefficients are awkward. The formula is also useful when a question asks you to "solve" rather than "express in the form."

**How do I spot a hidden quadratic?**

Look for three terms where the power of the first is twice the power of the second. If you see $\sin^2 x$ alongside $\sin x$, or $4^x$ alongside $2^x$, or $x^4$ alongside $x^2$ -- it's a hidden quadratic. Let $u$ equal the lower-power term.

**How many marks are quadratics worth directly?**

5-8 marks out of 75 (7-11%). But quadratic techniques appear embedded in 30 or more marks of other questions, making the true contribution closer to 40% of the paper.

**What's the most common quadratic mistake in 9709?**

Sign errors when completing the square with a negative leading coefficient. Cambridge examiners cite this in their reports every session. The fix is to expand your answer back out to verify.
