# A Level Maths Revision: The Complete Guide for 2026

A Level Maths isn't like other subjects. You can't revise it by re-reading notes. You can't summarise your way to understanding integration. The only way to revise maths is to do maths -- but doing it randomly wastes half your time.

That's the trap most students fall into. They open a textbook, skim through a chapter they already half-understand, do a few easy questions, and close the book feeling productive. Three weeks later, they sit a past paper and realise they've retained almost nothing.

This guide is different. It covers how to structure your A Level maths revision from start to finish -- what to prioritise, how to use past papers properly, which resources actually work, and the mistakes that cost marks every year. Whether you're sitting Cambridge 9709, Edexcel IAL, AQA, OCR, or any other A Level maths specification, the core revision strategy is the same.

The students who do well in A Level Maths aren't smarter. They're more strategic. Here's the strategy.

## Why Maths Revision Is Different From Every Other Subject

Effective A Level maths revision means spending the majority of your study time actively solving problems under timed conditions, diagnosing your weakest topics through past papers, and using spaced repetition to retain what you have learnt. It is not passive re-reading -- it is structured, diagnostic practice.

Priya spent three weeks before her Cambridge 9709 exam re-reading her class notes every evening. She highlighted key formulas. She rewrote definitions. She felt prepared. Then she opened the exam paper and could not complete a single integration question from start to finish.

Her problem wasn't effort. It was method.

Maths is a performance subject -- closer to learning a musical instrument than to learning history. You don't get better by reading about it. You get better by doing it, getting it wrong, understanding why, and doing it again. Research by [Roediger and Butler (2011)](https://psycnet.apa.org/doi/10.1016/j.jarmac.2012.06.003) found that active recall -- testing yourself on material rather than re-reading it -- improves retention by two to three times compared to passive review.

There are three distinct revision modes, and most students only use the first:

1. **Learning** -- reading notes, watching videos, building initial understanding
2. **Practising** -- working through questions by topic until techniques become fluent
3. **Testing** -- completing full past papers under timed exam conditions

The mistake is spending 80% of your time in mode one, when 80% of your marks come from modes two and three.

## Which Exam Board Are You On?

Before you plan your revision, you need to know exactly what you are revising for. A Level Maths varies significantly between exam boards -- different papers, different formula provisions, different question styles.

### Cambridge International (9709)

Cambridge 9709 is taken in over 160 countries and is one of the most widely sat A Level maths specifications in the world. The qualification consists of:

- **Pure Mathematics 1 (P1)** -- the compulsory foundation paper
- **Pure Mathematics 3 (P3)** -- the advanced pure paper
- **Mechanics (M1)** or **Statistics (S1)** -- your chosen applied module

The single most important thing to know about Cambridge 9709: **Pure 1 has no formula booklet**. You must memorise every formula -- quadratic, trigonometric identities, differentiation rules, integration results, all of it. This is a fundamentally different revision challenge from boards that provide a formula sheet.

If you're sitting Cambridge 9709, read our [complete Pure 1 revision guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/) for topic-by-topic breakdowns. For exam-specific strategy, see our guide on [how to pass Cambridge 9709](/cambridge/9709/how-to-pass/).

### Edexcel International A Level (IAL)

Edexcel IAL (specification codes WMA11, WMA12, and applied modules) is the main alternative for international students. Key differences from Cambridge 9709:

- **Formula booklet provided** in every exam -- you still need fluency, but memorisation pressure is lower
- **Two pure papers** (Pure 1 and Pure 2) rather than P1 and P3
- **Different question style** -- Edexcel tends toward longer, multi-part structured questions

The revision strategy in this guide applies to both Cambridge and Edexcel IAL. Where the approach differs, we will flag it.

### UK Boards (AQA, OCR, Edexcel UK)

If you are sitting A Level Maths with a UK board (AQA 7357, OCR A H240, or Edexcel Pearson UK), the core revision principles here still apply. UK specifications all provide formula booklets and follow a broadly similar syllabus structure with pure and applied components.

The main resources for UK board students are Physics and Maths Tutor (past papers and mark schemes), Maths Genie (graded topic questions), and your board's own specimen papers. This guide focuses primarily on Cambridge 9709 and Edexcel IAL, but the revision methodology works across all boards.

## What to Revise First: The Priority Framework

Most students revise in syllabus order. They start with chapter one and work through to the end. This feels logical but it is deeply inefficient.

Here is a better approach.

### The diagnostic method

Take one full past paper under timed conditions. Don't revise beforehand -- the point is to find out what you actually know right now, not what you knew last month. Mark it using the official mark scheme, then categorise every mark you lost by topic.

James, a student in Dubai sitting Cambridge 9709, did this eight weeks before his exam. He scored 42 out of 75 on his diagnostic Pure 1 paper. When he broke down the lost marks by topic, the picture was clear:

- Integration: 12 marks lost
- Trigonometry: 8 marks lost
- Coordinate geometry: 7 marks lost
- Functions: 4 marks lost
- Quadratics: 2 marks lost (see our [Cambridge 9709 quadratics guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/quadratics) for common pitfalls)

His revision plan wrote itself. Integration and trigonometry alone accounted for 20 of his 33 lost marks. Spending 60% of his revision time on those two topics would yield far more marks than spreading his time evenly across everything.

### The 80/20 rule for A Level maths revision

Across most A Level maths specifications, calculus (differentiation and integration combined) accounts for 35-40% of the pure mathematics marks. If you can only revise three topics thoroughly, make them:

1. **Integration** -- the highest mark-value topic on most papers. See our [Cambridge 9709 integration guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/integration) for worked examples and common question types.
2. **Differentiation** -- foundational to integration and frequently tested on its own. See our [Cambridge 9709 differentiation guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/differentiation) for the key techniques.
3. **Trigonometry** -- appears in pure papers and feeds into integration by substitution in later modules. Our [Cambridge 9709 trigonometry guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/trigonometry) covers the identities and question types you need.

This isn't a shortcut. It's prioritisation. Revise everything eventually, but start where the marks are.

## The A Level Maths Revision Timeline

How you revise depends entirely on how much time you have. Here are four scenarios, from ideal to emergency.

### 12 Weeks Out (The Ideal Start)

This is the revision timeline that produces the best results. It gives you enough time to learn, practise, and test properly.

**Phase 1: Topic mastery (Weeks 1-6)**

Dedicate one week to each of your weakest topics (identified by your diagnostic paper). For each topic:

- Day 1-2: Review notes or watch explanatory videos. Understand the theory.
- Day 3-4: Work through 15-20 graded questions, starting easy and building to exam-level difficulty.
- Day 5-6: Attempt past paper questions on that topic only. Use [a level maths questions by topic](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/) to find targeted practice.
- Day 7: Mark everything, review errors, make flashcards for formulas or methods you keep forgetting.

**Phase 2: Full papers (Weeks 7-10)**

Move to full past papers. Do two or three per week, always timed, always marked with the official mark scheme. After each paper, categorise your errors (more on this below).

**Phase 3: Gap-fill and peak (Weeks 11-12)**

By now you know your remaining weak spots. Target them with focused topic practice. Do one final past paper in the last week to confirm your readiness. Stop revising the night before the exam -- sleep matters more.

### 8 Weeks Out

Compress Phase 1 into four weeks instead of six. Cover your three weakest topics in the first four weeks, then run the full Phase 2 past paper programme. Skip Phase 3 if needed -- the past papers will reveal and fix your gaps simultaneously.

### 4 Weeks Out

Skip Phase 1 entirely. Go straight to past papers. Do one every two days. After each paper, spend the following day doing targeted practice on your weakest topic from that paper. This is less thorough but still effective.

### 2 Weeks Out

Past papers only. One per day if possible. Focus on exam technique -- timing, question selection, showing working for method marks. Don't try to learn new content. Consolidate what you already know.

## How to Use Past Papers Properly

Past papers are the most powerful revision tool you have. They are also the most misused.

### When to start

Don't start past papers until you've revised the underlying topics. Attempting a full paper when you haven't yet studied integration is demoralising and unproductive. Learn the topics first (Phase 1), then test with full papers (Phase 2).

The exception is your initial diagnostic paper. That one should be done cold, before any revision, to identify your starting point.

### How to do them

Every past paper should be done under full exam conditions. Good maths exam technique is built through realistic practice, not shortcuts:

- **Timed** -- 110 minutes for Cambridge 9709 P1, or whatever your paper's allocation is. No pausing.
- **Closed book** -- no notes, no formula sheet (unless your board provides one in the real exam)
- **Uninterrupted** -- phone off, door closed. Treat it as the real thing.

Fatimah, a student in Kuala Lumpur, initially did past papers in 30-minute blocks between other tasks. Her scores plateaued at around 55%. When she switched to full timed sittings, her scores jumped to 68% within two weeks -- not because she knew more maths, but because she had practised the stamina and time management the real exam demands.

### How to mark them

Always use the official mark scheme, not your own judgement. Then categorise every mark lost into one of three types:

1. **Knowledge gap** -- you did not know the method or formula. This requires going back to learn the topic.
2. **Execution error** -- you knew the method but made an algebraic or arithmetic mistake. This requires more careful practice.
3. **Reading error** -- you misunderstood what the question was asking. This requires practising question interpretation.

Each type has a different fix. Most students treat all lost marks the same way and revise the entire topic again. That wastes time. If you lost marks to execution errors on integration, you don't need to re-learn integration -- you need to practise being more precise.

### How many past papers should you do?

Aim for five to eight full papers per component. That means five to eight P1 papers, five to eight P3 papers, and so on. This gives you enough repetition to see the patterns in how topics are examined without running out of papers.

## Common A Level Maths Revision Mistakes

These are the a level maths tips that separate students who hit their target grade from those who don't -- and the revision mistakes that cost marks every year.

### 1. Re-reading notes without practising

This is the single most common mistake and the most costly. Reading through notes creates a feeling of familiarity that's easily mistaken for understanding. You recognise the content when you see it, so you assume you could reproduce it in an exam. You almost certainly can't.

The fix: close your notes and try a question. If you can't do it, you've identified a genuine gap. If you can, move on.

### 2. Revising topics you already know

It feels productive to work through questions you can already solve. It isn't. Every hour spent on a topic you've mastered is an hour not spent on one you haven't.

The fix: use your diagnostic paper results. Spend 80% of your time on your three weakest topics.

### 3. Doing past papers without timing

An untimed past paper is a completely different exercise from a timed one. Without time pressure, you can think through every step carefully. In the exam, you can't. Practising without timing builds habits that don't transfer to exam conditions.

The fix: every past paper, every time, fully timed. No exceptions.

### 4. Not reading examiner reports

Examiner reports are published after every exam session. They tell you, in the examiners' own words, exactly where students lost marks and why. They're free, available on your exam board's website, and almost nobody reads them.

The fix: download the examiner report for every past paper you complete. Read the comments on the questions you got wrong. You'll often find that your mistake is the exact one the examiner flagged.

### 5. Cramming the night before

Research on sleep and memory consolidation is unambiguous: a good night's sleep before an exam is worth more than an extra three hours of revision. Your brain consolidates and organises information during sleep. Depriving it of that process actively degrades your performance.

The fix: stop revising by 7pm the evening before your exam. Eat well, sleep well, and trust your preparation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many hours should I revise maths per week?

A minimum of five to six hours per week per component (e.g., five to six hours for Pure 1, five to six hours for Pure 3). This should be spread across at least three sessions -- daily short sessions are more effective than one long weekly block.

### How many past papers should I do before the exam?

Five to eight full papers per component is the target. Do them timed and mark them properly. Quality of review matters more than quantity of papers.

### Is it too late to start revising four weeks before the exam?

No. Four weeks is enough time to make a meaningful difference, but you need to be strategic. Skip the textbook review and go straight to past papers. Use your results to identify the two or three topics costing you the most marks, and focus there. Read the four-weeks-out timeline above.

### What grade is 67% in A Level Maths?

Grade boundaries vary by session and board, but 67% typically falls in the B range for most A Level maths specifications. For Cambridge 9709, check the specific session's grade thresholds on the Cambridge International website -- they shift by five to ten percentage points between sessions.

### How many students get an A* in A Level Maths?

Approximately 16-17% of A Level Maths students achieve an A* across all boards. The figure is slightly higher for Cambridge 9709 in some sessions. An A* is challenging but achievable with the right revision strategy and enough practice.

### How do I make a revision timetable for A Level Maths?

Start by taking a diagnostic past paper to identify your weakest topics. Then allocate 60-70% of your revision time to your three weakest areas, with the remaining time spread across stronger topics and full past papers. Block out five to six hours per week per component, split across at least three sessions. Follow the 12-week timeline in this guide and adjust the phases based on how many weeks you have before your exam.

### Is a grade 7 at GCSE enough for A Level Maths?

Yes. A grade 7 (formerly an A) at GCSE is the recommended minimum for starting A Level Maths. You will find the jump in difficulty significant -- particularly with pure mathematics topics like calculus that are entirely new -- but a grade 7 means you have the algebraic foundation to succeed.
