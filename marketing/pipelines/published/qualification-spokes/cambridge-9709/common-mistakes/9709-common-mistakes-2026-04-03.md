# 9709 Common Mistakes: What Cambridge Examiners Flag Every Session

Every session, Cambridge publishes examiner reports for 9709. Every session, they flag the same mistakes. Not conceptual failures -- avoidable errors from students who understood the maths but lost marks on execution.

## How Cambridge Marks Your Paper -- M, A, and B Marks

Before looking at specific mistakes, you need to understand how marks are awarded. Most students think of marks as binary -- right answer gets marks, wrong answer gets zero. That is not how Cambridge 9709 works.

Cambridge uses three types of marks, and understanding them changes how you approach every question.

| Mark Type | What It Stands For | How It Works | What It Means for You |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **M** | Method | Awarded for using the correct mathematical approach, even if your final answer is wrong | Show your working. A wrong answer with correct method still earns M marks. |
| **A** | Accuracy | Awarded only when the preceding method mark has been earned and the result is correct | A marks depend on M marks. No working shown = no M mark = no A mark either. |
| **B** | Independent | Awarded for a specific correct result, statement, or intermediate value -- independent of other marks | These stand alone. A correct sketch, a stated formula, or a specific value. |

The critical insight: **M marks reward your method, not your answer.**

On a four-mark question structured as M1 A1 M1 A1, showing the correct approach for both steps earns you two marks even if arithmetic errors produce a wrong final answer. Skip the working and write only the answer -- even the correct answer -- and you risk earning zero. The examiner cannot verify your method if you do not show it.

Cambridge examiner reports from 2018 to 2024 consistently identify "insufficient working shown" as the most widespread issue across all papers. It is not a topic-specific mistake. It is a working-discipline problem that costs marks on every question.

This is why cambridge maths exam tips always start with the same advice: show every step. Not because teachers enjoy reading your working, but because the mark scheme is built to reward it.

## Pure 1 Common Mistakes (Topics 1.1-1.8)

These are the ten most consistently cited mistakes from Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 examiner reports, covering sessions from 2018 to 2024. Each one includes the examiner observation (paraphrased), why students make the error, and the specific fix.

1. No working shown (all topics)
2. Only one trigonometric solution given
3. Sign errors in differentiation
4. Forgetting the constant of integration (+C)
5. Discriminant condition confusion
6. Chain rule multiplier omitted
7. Completing the square sign errors
8. Premature rounding
9. Segment perimeter trap (circular measure)
10. Negative area not handled in integration

### Mistake 1 -- No Working Shown (All Topics)

**What examiners flag:** Cambridge examiner reports consistently note that candidates who show no working cannot gain method marks. This observation appears in every Pure 1 examiner report across every session from 2018 to 2024. It is the single most frequently cited issue.

**Why it happens:** Calculator dependence. Students reach for the calculator, get the right number, and write it down. Under time pressure, writing out algebraic steps feels like wasted effort. It is not.

**The fix:** Every algebraic step gets its own line. Every integral shows the integration step before evaluation. Every derivative shows the rule you applied. Adopt this discipline: if you cannot point to a line of working that justifies each mark on the question, you have not written enough.

A useful test -- could someone follow your solution without seeing the question? If the answer is no, add more working.

### Mistake 2 -- Only One Trigonometric Solution (Trigonometry)

**What examiners flag:** The 2018-2024 examiner reports repeatedly observe that many candidates find only one solution when the question asks for all solutions in a given interval. This is especially common when solving equations like sin x = 0.5 or cos 2x = -1 over [0, 360].

**Why it happens:** Students use the inverse function on their calculator, get one angle, and stop. They forget -- or never fully internalised -- that trigonometric equations typically have multiple solutions in a 360-degree interval.

**The fix:** Always ask yourself: "How many solutions should there be?" For a standard sin or cos equation in [0, 360], expect two solutions. For equations involving 2x or 3x, expect more. Sketch the graph or use a CAST diagram to identify every solution systematically.

For example, sin θ = 0.5 in [0, 360] gives θ = 30 and θ = 150. Writing only θ = 30 loses you a mark -- and the examiner report will describe it as an "incomplete solution set."

Our [trigonometry guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/trigonometry/) covers the CAST method and solution-counting in detail.

### Mistake 3 -- Sign Errors in Differentiation (Differentiation)

**What examiners flag:** The examiner reports from 2018-2024 describe sign errors when differentiating negative and fractional powers as "widespread." This is particularly common with expressions involving terms like 1/x or 1/x squared, where the negative power creates a minus sign that students miss or misplace.

**Why it happens:** Students attempt to differentiate roots and reciprocals directly without first converting to power notation. Differentiating 1/x squared as a fraction is error-prone. Differentiating x to the power of -2 is mechanical.

**The fix:** Always rewrite before differentiating.

- 1/x becomes x to the power of -1
- 1/x squared becomes x to the power of -2
- The square root of x becomes x to the power of 1/2

Then apply the power rule. Bring the power down, reduce by one. The signs follow naturally from the algebra rather than from mental arithmetic.

Our [differentiation guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/differentiation/) walks through this conversion process with worked examples.

### Mistake 4 -- Forgetting the Constant of Integration (Integration)

**What examiners flag:** Cambridge examiner reports consistently note that the constant of integration (+C) is "frequently omitted" in indefinite integrals. This costs an independent B mark on almost every indefinite integration question.

**Why it happens:** Students focus on the integration technique -- getting the power rule right, handling the coefficients -- and then move on to the next question. The +C feels like a formality. It is not. It carries its own mark.

**The fix:** Build a physical habit: the moment you finish integrating, write +C before doing anything else. Before simplifying, before substituting, before moving to the next line. Make it automatic.

In questions that ask you to find a particular solution (given a point on the curve), the +C is not just worth a mark -- it is essential to the method. Without it, you cannot form the equation to find C, and the entire solution collapses.

See our [integration guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/integration/) for a full treatment of definite and indefinite integrals.

### Mistake 5 -- Discriminant Condition Confusion (Quadratics and Coordinate Geometry)

**What examiners flag:** The 2018-2024 examiner reports note that candidates frequently use the wrong inequality when applying the discriminant. Students confuse the conditions for "two distinct real roots," "equal roots," and "no real roots" -- especially in coordinate geometry questions about tangent lines.

**Why it happens:** Three conditions look similar:
- b squared minus 4ac > 0 means two distinct real roots
- b squared minus 4ac = 0 means equal (repeated) roots
- b squared minus 4ac < 0 means no real roots

Students memorise one or two but misapply them under pressure, particularly when the question uses language like "the line is a tangent to the curve" (which requires equal roots, so b squared minus 4ac = 0).

**The fix:** Learn all three as a set, not individually. And attach the geometric meaning: a tangent touches the curve at exactly one point, which means one repeated root, which means the discriminant equals zero. If you can translate the question language into the correct discriminant condition, the algebra is straightforward.

### Mistake 6 -- Chain Rule Multiplier Omitted (Differentiation)

**What examiners flag:** Examiner reports from multiple sessions observe that candidates often omit the derivative of the inner function when applying the chain rule. The 2023-2024 reports specifically note this as a recurring error in questions involving composite functions.

**Why it happens:** When differentiating an expression like (2x + 3) to the power of 4, students correctly bring the power down to get 4(2x + 3) cubed -- but forget to multiply by the derivative of the inner function (2x + 3), which is 2. The correct answer is 8(2x + 3) cubed, not 4(2x + 3) cubed.

**The fix:** After applying the power rule to the outer function, always ask: "What is inside the brackets, and what is its derivative?" Write that multiplier explicitly. If the inner function is (ax + b), its derivative is a. If it is something more complex, differentiate it separately and write it as a factor.

A reliable working pattern:
1. Differentiate the outer function as if the bracket were a single variable
2. Write a multiplication sign
3. Differentiate the inner function

This three-step layout makes the chain rule multiplier impossible to forget.

For more on the chain rule, see our [differentiation guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/differentiation/).

### Mistake 7 -- Completing the Square Sign Errors (Quadratics and Circles)

**What examiners flag:** The 2018-2024 examiner reports frequently cite sign errors in completing the square, both in pure quadratics questions and when converting circle equations to centre-radius form. The error typically occurs when halving the coefficient of x and determining the sign of the constant term.

**Why it happens:** Completing the square involves several small steps, and a sign error in any one of them cascades through the rest. Students often write x squared + 6x as (x + 3) squared + 9 instead of (x + 3) squared - 9. The subtraction of the squared half-coefficient is the step that trips most people.

**The fix:** Use a consistent method and check by expansion. If you write x squared + 6x = (x + 3) squared - 9, expand (x + 3) squared to verify: x squared + 6x + 9 - 9 = x squared + 6x. Correct.

That expansion check takes ten seconds and catches the most common error in the technique. Build it into your routine for every completing-the-square question.

Our [quadratics guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/quadratics/) covers this method in depth.

### Mistake 8 -- Premature Rounding (All Topics)

**What examiners flag:** Cambridge examiner reports consistently note that candidates who round intermediate values prematurely lose accuracy marks on the final answer. This appears across all Pure 1 topics but is especially costly in trigonometry and coordinate geometry, where calculations chain through multiple steps.

**Why it happens:** Students round to three significant figures at an intermediate step because "that's what the question asks for." But the question asks for three significant figures in the final answer, not at every stage. Rounding at step two and using that rounded value in steps three and four introduces cumulative error.

**The fix:** Two rules:
1. If the question says "give your answer in exact form," use surds, fractions, and multiples of pi throughout. No decimals at any stage.
2. If the question asks for a specific number of significant figures or decimal places, carry at least four significant figures through all intermediate working. Only round at the very end.

Write "= 3.46 (3 s.f.)" at the final step to show the examiner you rounded deliberately, not prematurely.

### Mistake 9 -- The Segment Perimeter Trap (Circular Measure)

**What examiners flag:** The examiner reports from 2018-2024 observe that candidates frequently calculate the perimeter of a segment as just the arc length, forgetting to add the chord. This is one of the most predictable a level maths common mistakes in the circular measure topic.

**Why it happens:** Students learn the arc length formula (s = r times theta) and apply it correctly. But a segment is bounded by an arc and a chord, not just an arc.

The perimeter of a segment is arc length plus chord length. Under time pressure, students write the arc length and move on, losing a mark for the missing chord.

**The fix:** Draw the diagram. Always. When you sketch a segment, you can see that it is bounded by two things: the curved arc and the straight chord. Both need to appear in your perimeter calculation.

For the chord length, use the cosine rule or the formula 2r sin(theta/2), depending on what the question gives you. Our [circular measure guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/circular-measure/) covers arc, sector, and segment calculations in full.

Nadia lost three marks on a circular measure question in her March mock. Not because she could not do the maths -- she calculated the arc length perfectly. But she wrote only the arc length as the perimeter, missing the chord entirely.

She knew the formula. She just did not draw the diagram, so the missing chord was invisible. One sketch would have saved those marks. After that paper, she adopted a rule: every circular measure question starts with a labelled diagram. No exceptions.

### Mistake 10 -- Negative Area Not Handled (Integration)

**What examiners flag:** The 2018-2024 examiner reports note that candidates frequently fail to account for regions below the x-axis when finding areas by integration. When a curve crosses the x-axis within the limits, the definite integral returns a negative value for the portion below.

Students who integrate straight through without splitting at the x-intercept get a smaller area than the actual answer. Some leave a negative result as their final answer.

**Why it happens:** The definite integral gives the signed area, not the geometric area. If you integrate a function that dips below the x-axis between your limits, the negative portion partially cancels the positive portion. Students who learned "integration gives you the area" without the nuance about signed area get caught.

**The fix:**
1. Find where the curve crosses the x-axis within your limits of integration
2. Split the integral at those x-intercepts
3. Integrate each portion separately
4. Take the absolute value of each result
5. Add them together

This gives the total geometric area. Our [integration guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/integration/) covers the negative area trap with worked diagrams.

## How to Stop Losing Marks to These Mistakes

Knowing the mistakes is the first step. Stopping yourself from making them requires a change in habits, not just knowledge. Here are two methods that work.

### The Error Log Method

After every practice paper, go through every mark you lost and categorise the error:

- **Knowledge gap** -- you did not know the method or the formula. Fix: revise the topic. Use the relevant section of our [Pure 1 Revision Guide](/cambridge/9709/pure-1/).
- **Execution error** -- you knew the method but made a sign error, forgot +C, dropped the chain rule multiplier, or rounded too early. Fix: add it to your error log with the specific mistake and the question reference.
- **Reading error** -- you answered a different question than the one asked. You gave decimals when the question said "exact form." You found one solution when it asked for all solutions. Fix: underline key instruction words in the question before you start.

The error log is the important part. After three or four practice papers, you will see patterns. If sign errors in differentiation appear three times, that is not carelessness -- it is a systematic habit you can target.

Track the date, paper, question number, mark type lost (M or A), the specific error, and the topic. Over time, this log becomes the most efficient revision tool you own -- because it shows you exactly what to practise.

Daniel started his error log in February, six weeks before his May exam. After three practice papers, a pattern emerged: he had lost marks on sign errors in differentiation four times, and forgotten the constant of integration twice.

He was not weak on calculus. He had a specific, fixable execution habit. He spent one focused session on converting to power notation before differentiating and forcing himself to write +C immediately after every integral. By his fifth practice paper, those errors had stopped completely.

His mock score went from 58 to 67 out of 75. The maths had not changed. The habits had.

### The Working Discipline

Clean, structured working is not just about earning method marks. It also reduces errors, because each line is simple enough to check.

Build these habits:

- **One algebraic step per line.** Do not combine two operations in one step. If you are substituting and then simplifying, those are two separate lines.
- **Label what you are doing.** Write "differentiating:" or "substituting x = 2:" or "applying chain rule:" before the step. This forces you to think about what you are doing, not just do it on autopilot.
- **Check by substitution.** After solving for x, substitute your answer back into the original equation. After integrating, differentiate your answer to check it matches the original expression. These take seconds and catch most arithmetic errors.

The question to ask yourself: could someone follow your working without seeing the question? If the answer is no, your working is not detailed enough for the mark scheme.
