# ExamPilot Parent Voice Guide

Voice for all parent-facing content: "For Parents" landing page, parent email sequences, Facebook Group posts, WhatsApp parent messages, pricing page trust elements, and checkout flow. This is NOT the student voice (see `voice-house.md`).

---

## The Voice in One Sentence

A knowledgeable guide who speaks to parents as partners in their child's academic success. Professional, trustworthy, outcomes-focused.

---

## Five Voice Pillars

### 1. Trust and Credentials
Lead with credibility. Parents are making a financial decision about their child's education. They need to know ExamPilot is built by people who understand the exam, not just the technology.

**Do:** Reference exam board specifications, learning science methodology, GDPR compliance, data safety.
**Avoid:** Hype, unsubstantiated claims, startup language ("disrupting education").

### 2. Value Justification
Frame pricing against what parents already spend. EUR29/month is 1-2 hours of private tutoring. EUR144/year is a fraction of school fees (0.5-7% across all target markets). Parents respond to concrete comparisons, not abstract value statements.

**Do:** Compare with tutoring costs in their market. Show what they get for the price.
**Avoid:** Apologising for the price. Discounting language. "Cheap" or "affordable" (position as good value, not low cost).

### 3. Progress Visibility
Parents want to see that their investment is working. Lead with what parents can track: topic-level progress, Exam Readiness Index trends, session frequency, knowledge gap identification.

**Do:** Show the parent dashboard concept. Emphasise that progress is measurable, not subjective.
**Avoid:** Overpromising grade improvements. "Guaranteed results."

### 4. Safety and Privacy
This is non-negotiable for parents of under-18s. GDPR compliance, no social features, no chat with strangers, pure study tool. Many parents in target markets are particularly protective of online activity.

**Do:** State GDPR compliance prominently. Explain data minimisation. Mention no social features.
**Avoid:** Burying privacy information. Assuming parents don't care about data.

### 5. Student Success Focus
Parents want outcomes: better grades, university readiness, confidence. Frame everything through the lens of their child's success, not product features.

**Do:** "Your child will know exactly where they stand on Cambridge 9709."
**Avoid:** Feature lists without benefit translation. Technical jargon (FSRS, Question DNA) without explaining what it means for their child.

## Messaging Framework

### Core Value Proposition (Parent)
"See exactly where your child stands. Know what they need to work on. Track their progress week by week."

### Key Messages

**Message 1 -- Visibility**
"ExamPilot's Exam Readiness Index shows you precisely how prepared your child is for each topic. No more 'I think I revised enough.'"

**Message 2 -- Value**
"From EUR12/month with an annual plan. That's one hour of private tutoring for a year of daily practice. ExamPilot works alongside tutoring, not instead of it."

**Message 3 -- Safety**
"Built for under-18s. GDPR compliant. No social features, no chat, no distractions. A pure study tool focused on exam readiness."

**Message 4 -- Specificity**
"Built specifically for Cambridge International 9709 and Edexcel IAL mathematics. Every question maps to the actual exam specification your child is sitting."

## CTAs (Parent-Facing)

- "See how ExamPilot tracks your child's progress"
- "Start a free trial for your child"
- "Learn how ExamPilot prepares students for Cambridge 9709"
- "See what other parents are saying"

### CTA Rules (Parent)
- Trust signal before every CTA (GDPR badge, money-back mention, or testimonial)
- Never pressure. Parents making education decisions resist urgency tactics.
- "Free trial" always includes "no credit card needed" if applicable
- First CTA on parent pages should be informational ("learn more"), not transactional

---

## What We Never Do (Parent Content)

- Guilt framing ("Don't let your child fall behind")
- Comparison with other parents' choices
- Pressure tactics or artificial urgency
- Promising specific grade improvements
- Undermining tutors or teachers
- "AI tutor", "AI-powered", or any AI-forward language (parents are more skeptical of AI in education than students)
- B2B school licensing language
- Showing student data without explaining consent model
- Using student-casual voice (no slang, no peer-level register)
