# ExamPilot Brand Voice & Style Guide

Shared voice for all ExamPilot external content: blog articles, landing pages, social posts, email, and product copy. Reference this when writing any content that goes out under the ExamPilot brand.

For internal team outputs (Coda docs, decisions, communications), the defaults below still apply but with a looser register.

---

## The Voice in One Sentence

A sharp, slightly older student who nailed their A-Levels and is now showing you exactly how — direct, confident, practical, no time wasted.

---

## Five Voice Pillars

### 1. Confident Guide, Not Distant Expert
Direct and assured. Say "here's what to do" not "you might want to consider." No hedging, no false modesty.

**Avoid:** Academic language, condescension, wishy-washy advice that doesn't commit.

**Cultural note:** Our students span the UK, EU, UAE, Pakistan, Malaysia, Singapore, Nigeria, and beyond. Confidence translates universally. Arrogance does not.

### 2. Relentlessly Practical
Every sentence earns its place by being useful. Action-oriented, specific, step-by-step. Numbers, methods, examples — not vague encouragement.

**Avoid:** Motivational fluff without substance, generic study tips recyclable from any site, filler content.

### 3. Honest About the Challenge
Don't pretend A-Level Maths is easy. Acknowledge the difficulty, then show the path through it. Students trust us because we don't bullshit them.

**Avoid:** Toxic positivity, minimising difficulty, promising unrealistic results.

### 4. Data-Driven, Not Data-Drowning
Use numbers and scores to back claims — but always translate them into what they mean for the student. Show the data, then tell the story.

**Avoid:** Metrics without context, hiding behind numbers instead of giving clear guidance.

### 5. Student-First, Always
Students are the primary audience. Every piece of student-facing content asks: "Does this help a 16-18 year old study better?" Parents are the economic buyer in many target markets. Teachers are referral partners. Neither replaces students as the centre of the product and its voice.

**Avoid:** Talking about students in third person in student-facing content, corporate speak, trying too hard to be "cool."

## Messaging Framework

### Core Value Propositions

**For A-Level Maths Students (primary):**
"Stop guessing if you're ready. Know exactly where you stand and what to do next."

**For Resitting Students:**
"Last time didn't go as planned. This time, you'll know exactly what to fix."

**For Cambridge 9709 Students:**
"Cambridge 9709 prep built by people who understand the exam — not just the maths."

**For Tutors:**
"See exactly where each student is struggling and spend your time where it matters most."

### Key Messages

**Message 1 — Know Where You Stand**
The Exam Readiness Index (ERI) gives a precise, data-backed picture of exam readiness — not a guess. Topic-level breakdown shows exactly which areas need work.

**Message 2 — Practice That Works**
Adaptive difficulty, Question DNA targeting the reasoning patterns behind exam questions, spaced repetition so retention holds.

**Message 3 — Built for YOUR Exam**
Every question maps to the Cambridge 9709 or Edexcel IAL specification. Not generic maths — the actual exam.

**Message 4 — AI Study Partner**
Ask Sparky is Socratic — it guides through questions, not answers. Available in every practice session, understands the specific question being worked on.

## Formatting Standards

### Sentence Structure
- Short punchy sentences for impact. Longer ones to explain methodology.
- Active voice: "ExamPilot identifies your weak spots" not "Weak spots are identified by ExamPilot"
- Average sentence: 12-18 words (students read on phones — scan-optimised)
- Front-load the value in every sentence

### Paragraphs
- 1-3 sentences. Rarely more.
- One idea per paragraph.
- Line breaks generously — white space is useful on mobile.

### Punctuation
- Oxford comma: yes, always
- Em dashes: no. Replace with comma, semicolon, or period.
- Exclamation marks: maximum one per article. We're confident, not excited.
- Ellipsis: avoid in published content.

### UK English
- colour, analyse, organise, practise (verb)/practice (noun)
- "revision" not "review" for exam prep context
- Avoid UK-specific idioms international students won't understand ("swotting")

### Capitalisation
- ExamPilot (always: capital E and P)
- Ask Sparky (both words)
- Exam Readiness Index, ERI (first mention with abbreviation)
- Question DNA, Smart Review, ExamLens (both words)
- A-Level (always hyphenated, capital A and L)
- Cambridge 9709, Pure 1, Pure 2 (capital P, numeral not spelled out)
- Edexcel IAL, WMA11, WMA12 (all as shown)

### Numbers
- Spell out one through nine in prose
- Numerals for 10 and above
- Always numerals for: grades (A*, A, B), percentages, scores, paper numbers

## CTAs

### Pre-launch
- "Join the waitlist" (primary)
- "Check your exam readiness"
- "See how ExamPilot works"

### Post-launch
- "Try ExamPilot free"
- "Start your free trial — no credit card needed"

### Content CTAs (mid-article)
- "Explore our Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 revision guide"
- "See all past papers for 9709 Paper 1"
- "Find your knowledge gaps in 10 minutes"

### CTA Rules
- Never more than 3 CTAs per article
- First CTA within first 500 words (soft — learn more)
- Final CTA at end (strong — convert)
- Make each CTA contextual to the section it's in

## What We Never Do

- Clickbait headlines
- Unsubstantiated claims ("best app ever")
- "AI tutor", "AI wrapper", "AI-powered", "game-changing", "revolutionary"
- Thin content under 500 words as standalone articles
- Keyword stuffing
- Publish without human review
- B2B school licensing or institution pricing (consumer-only purchase model; see `voice-parent.md` and `voice-teacher.md` for non-student audiences)
- Pricing in GBP or USD (EUR only: EUR29/mo, EUR69/3mo, EUR96/6mo, EUR144/yr)
- Testimonials from under-18s without parental consent
